What Lowi Predicted: Redistributive Legislation and the Committee Processing Penalty in the Korean National Assembly

KNA Research Agents | AI-Generated Working Paper | 2026-03-28 Source: Forum Round 4 | Status: Experimental Draft

Abstract

Despite six decades of theoretical prediction that redistributive policies generate greater legislative friction than distributive policies (Lowi 1964), no study has tested this prediction at the committee processing stage, where the vast majority of legislation succeeds or fails. We provide a first such test using 15,291 bills from the Korean National Assembly (20th and 21st terms, 2016 - 2024). Classifying bills by policy type and beneficiary population, we find that redistributive livelihood legislation faces a 9.3 percentage-point committee processing penalty after controlling for arrival timing, sponsor characteristics, bill seriousness, and committee fixed effects (p < 0.001). The penalty operates within committees, within individual sponsors' portfolios, and across seriousness levels. Under divided government, it nearly triples to 18.4 percentage points, with labor and welfare bills absorbing the steepest decline. These findings connect foundational policy typology theory to observable legislative outcomes at the bill level.

1. Introduction

Roughly 80 percent of bills introduced in the Korean National Assembly never receive a committee decision. They are placed on a standing committee agenda, remain there for the duration of the four-year Assembly term, and expire without any recorded action - no vote, no revision, no formal rejection. This pattern of passive inaction, rather than active gatekeeping, constitutes the dominant mode of bill failure in one of Asia's most active legislatures, where bill introduction volume grew sixfold between the 17th Assembly (2004 - 2008) and the 21st Assembly (2020 - 2024).

The sheer scale of committee-stage attrition raises a distributional question that the existing literature has not addressed: does it matter which bills die? If committee inaction is content-blind - a mechanical consequence of limited processing bandwidth confronting exponentially growing bill volume - then the distributional profile of dead bills should mirror that of processed bills. But if certain types of legislation face systematically higher failure rates, committee inaction carries latent policy consequences even absent deliberate discrimination. In particular, if bills targeting workers, welfare recipients, care dependents, and other vulnerable populations face disproportionate committee-stage attrition, legislative paralysis functions as what Hacker (2004) calls "policy drift": the status quo is maintained precisely when policy adaptation is most needed, and populations most dependent on government action absorb the cost.

We argue that the committee processing penalty for redistributive legislation is real, substantial, and theoretically predictable from Lowi's (1964) foundational distinction between redistributive and distributive policy types. Redistributive legislation - minimum wage mandates, occupational safety requirements, welfare expansions - creates identifiable winners and losers, generating organized opposition that impedes committee consensus. Distributive legislation - targeted subsidies, tax exemptions, sector-specific support - creates beneficiaries without mobilizing organized opponents. This asymmetry should manifest as differential committee processing rates, even controlling for bill-level characteristics and committee workload.

Using bill-level data from the Korean National Assembly (KNA) database covering the 20th and 21st Assembly terms (2016 - 2024), we classify 15,291 bills by beneficiary population and Lowi type through keyword analysis of bill propose-reason texts. We then estimate logistic regression models with committee fixed effects, testing whether bill content predicts committee processing after controlling for arrival timing, cosponsor count, text length, sponsor party, and government regime. Three robustness strategies address the position-taking confound that plagues bill-level analyses: within-sponsor comparisons, seriousness-proxy stratification, and prolific-sponsor diagnostics.

This paper makes three contributions. First, we provide what is, to our knowledge, the first test of Lowi's redistributive-distributive prediction at the committee processing stage in any legislature. The prediction is confirmed with striking precision: redistributive labor bills receive committee decisions at a rate of 22.8 percent, compared to 38.5 percent for distributive labor bills - a 15.7 percentage-point gap within the same policy domain. Second, we extend the content-classified bill fate analysis pioneered by Volden, Wiseman, and Wittmer (2016) with committee-level decomposition, partisan-regime interaction, and capacity-constraint mechanisms that their U.S. Congressional study does not address. Third, we document that divided government nearly triples the content-based processing penalty, connecting the institutional literature on gridlock (Binder 2003; Tsebelis 2002) to bill-level distributional consequences.

2. Literature and Theory

Committee winnowing and the content-blindness assumption

The dominant framework for understanding committee-stage bill attrition is Krutz's (2005, doi:10.1111/j.0092-5853.2005.00125.x) winnowing model, which characterizes committee gatekeeping as a necessary institutional response to bill volume exceeding processing capacity. Under winnowing, committees triage bills based on arrival order, sponsor characteristics, and workload constraints rather than policy content. The implication is that committee inaction is distributionally neutral: the bills that die are those that arrived too late or lacked sufficient political support, not those with particular policy orientations.

We complicate this framework by introducing a content dimension. Even if committees do not deliberately screen bills by topic, the political dynamics that Lowi (1964) identified may produce differential processing rates across policy types. Redistributive policies - those that impose costs on identifiable groups to benefit others - generate organized opposition from both sides, making committee consensus harder to achieve. Distributive policies, by contrast, target benefits to specific constituencies without creating organized losers. Regulatory policies fall between. There exists, to our knowledge, no study testing this foundational prediction at the committee processing stage, despite its direct implications for how legislative institutions filter policy demands.

Content-classified bill fates

The closest precedent for our approach is Volden, Wiseman, and Wittmer (2016, doi:10.1017/psrm.2016.32), who classify U.S. House bills by content (women's issues) and track their legislative fates. They find that women's issues bills have a 2 percent passage rate, compared to 4 percent for all bills. Our project extends their design in three ways: we decompose the processing penalty into committee-stage and floor-stage components, we test whether the penalty varies by partisan regime, and we identify a capacity-constraint mechanism through which overloaded committees disproportionately stall redistributive legislation.
