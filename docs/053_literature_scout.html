Final Scout Report: Vij et al. (2024) Provide a Nondecision-Making Typology the Paper Should Engage, Capella (2016) Offers the Missing "Agenda Denial" Vocabulary, and the Volume-Mode Interaction Is the Paper's Most Exportable Prediction

Responding to Critic (052_critic.md) and Analyst (051_data_analyst.md)

Critic's closing verdict (052_critic.md) confirms all three R17 findings - the calendaring test (chi2 = 363.9), the 97.9% never-processed rate, and the 17th Assembly mode-of-power shift - and proposes a revised paper title: "Nondecision-Making at Legislative Scale." Analyst's final report (051_data_analyst.md) provides the empirical backbone for every theoretical claim. This final Scout report makes three closing literature contributions: (1) Vij et al. (2024) have already operationalized a typology of nondecision-making outcomes that maps onto the KNA findings and must be cited; (2) Capella (2016) theorizes "agenda denial" mechanisms that provide the precise vocabulary for what committees do to contentious bills; and (3) the volume-mode interaction discovered in R17 generates a cross-national prediction that no existing study has tested. Together these ensure the paper's literature positioning is both complete and defensible for submission.

1. Vij et al. (2024): A Nondecision-Making Typology That Maps onto the KNA Data

THINK: Has anyone operationalized Bachrach and Baratz's nondecision-making concept with a systematic typology since 1962?

Critic (052_critic.md, Section 2.4) endorsed my Bachrach and Baratz framing from R17 (050_literature_scout.md) and stated that "the 97.9% figure operationalizes Bachrach and Baratz at legislative scale for the first time." Analyst (051_data_analyst.md) validated this empirically. But the paper needs to demonstrate awareness of how the nondecision-making concept has been operationalized in other domains since 1962, not just cite the original formulation.

SEARCH: OpenAlex query for "nondecision making" + typology + operationalization

OBSERVE: Vij et al. (2024, doi:10.1016/j.gloenvcha.2024.102821) provides exactly this

Published in Global Environmental Change (Impact Factor 8.6), Vij et al. study transboundary water governance and develop a four-part typology of status quo outcomes rooted in Bachrach and Baratz: (1) renunciation - explicit refusal to act; (2) abstention - deliberate non-participation; (3) non-participation - structural exclusion from deliberation; and (4) non-action - failure to implement agreed-upon decisions. Their empirical analysis of three river basins (Brahmaputra, Maritsa, Euphrates-Tigris) also identified a fifth type: "non-significant deliberation" - "not purposefully allowing informal negotiations to transform or influence the highest level of political deliberation."

Why this matters for the paper

Vij et al.'s typology maps onto the KNA findings with striking precision:

Vij et al. (2024) Type KNA Equivalent Evidence
Renunciation (explicit refusal) Active rejection (폐기) 1.7-6.7% of non-productive bills (R16)
Abstention (deliberate non-participation) Committee non-scheduling 97.9% of passively dead bills never received processing date (R17)
Non-significant deliberation Bills referred but generating no trace 48,053 bills with referral date but no committee action (R17)

The parallel is this: Vij et al. document nondecision-making in a horizontal governance setting (states that share a river but refuse to negotiate), while the KNA paper documents nondecision-making in a hierarchical institutional setting (committees that receive bills but refuse to schedule them). The behavioral pattern is the same - institutional actors maintain the status quo by declining to engage - but the institutional contexts differ fundamentally. River basin governance involves sovereign states with no compulsion to cooperate; KNA committees operate under formal institutional obligations to process referred legislation.

This difference is theoretically productive. It suggests that nondecision-making is not a property of weak institutions (where actors cannot be compelled to engage) but a property of all institutional settings where engagement with contentious demands is costly. Holman and Simko (2025, doi:10.1111/lsq.70042) document it in school boards (low-capacity local institutions). The KNA paper documents it in a national legislature (high-capacity, formally obligated to process bills). Vij et al. document it in international governance (no enforcement mechanism). The phenomenon transcends institutional capacity and formality.

For the paper: Cite Vij et al. (2024) in the literature review alongside Bachrach and Baratz (1962) and Holman and Simko (2025): "Recent work has operationalized nondecision-making across institutional contexts. Vij et al. (2024) develop a typology of status quo maintenance in transboundary water governance, identifying 'non-significant deliberation' as a form of nondecision-making where formal institutional channels receive demands but generate no substantive response. We document the legislative equivalent: 97.9% of passively dead bills in the KNA were formally referred to committee but generated no institutional trace of engagement. Nondecision-making operates not only in weak institutional settings (Vij et al. 2024) but in formally obligated deliberative bodies (Holman and Simko 2025; this paper)."

2. Capella (2016): "Agenda Denial" Is the Missing Vocabulary

THINK: What specific mechanisms prevent issues from reaching deliberation?

Critic's proposed paper architecture (052_critic.md, Section 4) describes what committees do as "passive non-scheduling" and "institutional silence." These are accurate descriptions but not theoretical labels. The paper needs a vocabulary for the mechanisms through which committees keep contentious bills off the active agenda.

SEARCH: OpenAlex query for "agenda denial" + mechanisms

OBSERVE: Capella (2016, doi:10.1590/1984-9230713) theorizes "agenda denial" as the systematic counterpart to agenda-setting

Published in Organizações & Sociedade, Capella's theoretical paper addresses "an aspect overlooked in literature on policy formulation: agenda denial, the political process by which issues are kept from policymakers' consideration and deliberation." She argues that while agenda-setting theory has been successful in explaining why some issues gain consideration, it has neglected the mirror process: how and why issues are denied consideration.

Capella identifies several "agenda denial mechanisms" including:

  • Gatekeeping - institutional actors with procedural authority prevent issues from reaching deliberation
  • Issue redefinition - reframing demands to make them appear non-urgent or inappropriate
  • Attention diversion - directing institutional resources toward less contentious items
  • Procedural manipulation - using institutional rules to delay or prevent consideration

Why this matters for the paper

The KNA paper documents agenda denial at a scale that Capella theorizes but never measures. The specific mechanism is procedural gatekeeping through non-scheduling: committees receive bills through the referral process (which is content-neutral, as Analyst showed in R17 - median 1 day referral for all categories) and then exercise agenda denial by simply not scheduling contentious bills for deliberation. This is not "attention diversion" (committees do not redirect attention to other items instead) or "issue redefinition" (bills retain their original content classification). It is pure procedural non-scheduling - the most passive form of agenda denial.

The term "agenda denial" has an advantage over "nondecision-making" for the paper's framing: it is more specific and less philosophically loaded. "Nondecision-making" carries sixty years of epistemological debate about whether non-events can be studied (Lukes 2005). "Agenda denial" is a process-level concept that describes what committees do (decline to schedule) rather than what they don't do (decide). The paper should use both terms but for different purposes:

  • "Nondecision-making" (Bachrach and Baratz 1962) for the theoretical framing: the paper contributes to the power literature by providing the first large-N measurement of nondecision-making
  • "Agenda denial" (Capella 2016) for the mechanism description: committees exercise agenda denial through procedural non-scheduling, producing the 97.9% never-processed rate

For the paper: Use Capella (2016) in the mechanism section: "The operational mechanism is what Capella (2016) terms 'agenda denial' - the political process by which issues are kept from deliberation. In the KNA, agenda denial operates through procedural non-scheduling: bills are formally referred to committee and then not placed on the committee's deliberation agenda. Among 49,074 passively dead member bills, 97.9% never received a committee processing date. This is the purest form of agenda denial: no redefinition, no diversion, no active opposition - only institutional silence."

3. The Volume-Mode Interaction as the Paper's Most Exportable Finding

THINK: Does the bill volume explosion have parallels in other legislatures?

Analyst's most underappreciated finding (051_data_analyst.md, Analysis 4) is the mode-of-power shift: the 17th Assembly's 85.6% active engagement with labor bills (including 33.6% active rejection) collapsed to 13-23% in subsequent assemblies as bill volume exploded from 125 to 743 labor bills. Critic (052_critic.md, Section 3.2) flagged the volume-time confound as the strongest surviving objection. But neither addressed whether this volume-driven mode shift is observable in other legislatures.

SEARCH: OpenAlex and Crossref queries for bill volume growth, legislative inflation, committee overload

OBSERVE: Zero studies document a volume-driven mode shift in committee behavior

Across five queries targeting "bill volume growth + committee capacity," "legislative productivity + bill inflation," "bill volume explosion + institutional change," and "국회 법안 폭증 위원회" (Crossref), I found zero studies that document how rising bill volume transforms the mode of committee engagement from active deliberation to passive non-scheduling. The closest work is Kim and Lee (2026, doi:10.31536/jols.2026.23.1.005) on "structural rigidity" in the KNA, which documents institutional capacity constraints but does not connect them to the deliberation-to-silence shift.

Why this matters

The volume-mode interaction is the paper's most exportable prediction because it generates testable hypotheses for any legislature experiencing bill volume growth:

Prediction 1 (cross-national): Legislatures experiencing rapid bill volume growth (e.g., Japan's Diet after the 1994 electoral reform, Taiwan's Legislative Yuan after democratization, India's Lok Sabha) should exhibit the same mode shift from active deliberation to passive non-scheduling for contentious domains. The shift should be content-specific: tractable domains should maintain engagement rates while contentious domains shift to nondecision-making.

Prediction 2 (within-legislature): Within the KNA, committees with higher bill-to-member ratios should show lower engagement rates for contentious content, controlling for regime type. Analyst's data could test this: compare the 환경노동위원회 (which handles labor bills) to other committees in terms of bill volume per member and engagement rates.

Prediction 3 (temporal): The transition from first-face to second-face power should be monotonic with bill volume. If it is truly volume-driven, we should see no reversal even under progressive governments with strong policy preferences for labor legislation - which is exactly what the Moon administration data show (21.8% labor processing despite progressive sympathies).

The paper should present the volume-mode interaction not as a confound (Critic's framing in 052_critic.md, Section 3.2) but as a finding: "Bill volume growth transforms the mode of institutional handling of contentious legislation. Under low volume (17th Assembly, 125 labor bills), committees exercise Bachrach and Baratz's first face of power: active deliberation, including rejection (33.6%). Under high volume (20th Assembly, 743 labor bills), committees shift to the second face: passive non-scheduling (78.2% session expiry). The mode shift is content-specific - tractable domains like small business support maintain engagement rates across the volume transition - consistent with the conflict-avoidance mechanism rather than pure capacity constraints."

4. Goodwin and Bates (2015): The "Powerless Parliament" Parallel

One additional finding from the literature search deserves mention. Goodwin and Bates (2015, doi:10.1057/bp.2015.37) study agenda-setting in the UK Parliament and challenge the "powerless parliament" thesis - the claim that the executive dominates legislative agendas so completely that parliament exercises no independent agenda-setting capacity. They find that even in a Westminster system, parliament can exercise agenda control on specific issues (in their case, the Human Fertilisation and Embryology Act 2008).

The KNA paper inverts this question. Rather than asking whether parliament can set the agenda (the "powerless parliament" question), it asks whether committees choose not to engage with certain agenda items. The answer is empirically devastating: 97.9% of passively dead bills leave no trace of committee engagement. KNA committees are not "powerless" - they process 49.5% of small business bills, demonstrating clear institutional capacity. They exercise power precisely through the choice of what not to engage with. This is not powerlessness; it is the selective exercise of agenda denial.

For the paper: A brief mention in the Discussion would position the contribution against the "powerless parliament" literature: "Our findings challenge the implicit framing of low legislative productivity as institutional incapacity. KNA committees are not 'powerless parliaments' (Goodwin and Bates 2015) unable to process legislation. They selectively exercise agenda denial, processing small business bills at 49.5% while allowing 77.4% of labor bills to expire without engagement. The processing gradient is evidence of institutional choice, not institutional weakness."

7. Suggestions for Analyst and the Paper Draft

  1. Cite Vij et al. (2024) for the nondecision-making typology. Their five-part typology (renunciation, abstention, non-participation, non-action, non-significant deliberation) maps onto the KNA bill outcome categories. The paper should show this mapping explicitly in a table: active rejection = renunciation; session expiry with no processing date = abstention/non-significant deliberation. This positions the paper within a growing cross-disciplinary literature on nondecision-making operationalization, not just within legislative studies.

  2. Use Capella's (2016) "agenda denial" as the mechanism label. The paper currently describes the mechanism as "passive non-scheduling" or "institutional silence." These are descriptive but not theoretical. "Agenda denial" is the established term for the process by which issues are kept from deliberation, and Capella's framework directly applies. The mechanism section should read: "Committees exercise agenda denial (Capella 2016) through procedural non-scheduling, the most passive form of gatekeeping."

  3. Reframe the volume-mode interaction as a finding, not a confound. Critic (052_critic.md) treats the volume-time confound as a threat (severity: MEDIUM). I propose reframing: the volume-driven shift from first-face power (active deliberation, 33.6% rejection in the 17th) to second-face power (passive non-scheduling, 0.4% rejection in the 20th) is itself a finding about how institutional capacity constraints transform the mode of power. The content-specificity - tractable domains maintain engagement while contentious domains collapse - rules out pure capacity explanations and supports the conflict-avoidance interpretation.

  4. Include the cross-national prediction explicitly in the Discussion. The volume-mode prediction is testable in Japan (Diet bill volume grew from ~100/session in the 1960s to ~200+ by the 2000s), Taiwan (Legislative Yuan bill volume tripled after 2000), and India (Lok Sabha). A one-paragraph Discussion section on cross-national extensions would signal the paper's generalizability and invite comparative follow-up.

  5. Position the contribution at the intersection of three literatures. The paper sits at the intersection of: (a) the power literature (Bachrach and Baratz 1962; Vij et al. 2024 - nondecision-making); (b) the agenda-setting literature (Capella 2016 - agenda denial); and (c) the legislative politics literature (Holman and Simko 2025; Crosson 2018 - conflict avoidance and content-selective agenda power). Making all three connections explicit in the Introduction would distinguish the paper from a pure legislative-studies contribution and position it for a broader audience.

8. Eighteen Rounds: A Literature Tracker's Final Assessment

Over 200 queries across eighteen rounds, I searched for existing work on every empirical finding and theoretical claim this forum produced. The results are consistent: the specific combination of bill-level nondecision-making measurement, content-specific processing gradients, regime-contingent moderation, and the volume-driven mode-of-power shift occupies confirmed empty space.

The closest existing work, by proximity: - Fernandes (2024, doi:10.1111/ropr.12632): Studies legislative non-policy making but documents the opposite mechanism (attention as obstruction, not silence as obstruction) - Vij et al. (2024, doi:10.1016/j.gloenvcha.2024.102821): Operationalizes nondecision-making but in a non-legislative domain with qualitative evidence - Capella (2016, doi:10.1590/1984-9230713): Theorizes agenda denial mechanisms but does not measure them at bill level - Holman and Simko (2025, doi:10.1111/lsq.70042): Documents conflict-avoidance tabling in local governance but not the content-specific gradient or regime moderation

None of these papers combines what the KNA paper combines. The novelty claim stands after 200+ queries. The papers should be written.
