Moderator Report

Yeouido Agora Discussion Report: AI Basic Act

1. Stimulus

Korea's AI Basic Act took effect on January 22, 2026, designating 10 "high-impact AI" sectors with safety evaluation and transparency requirements, but drew criticism from startups over compliance costs and from civil society for lacking outright prohibition clauses.

2. Sentiment Overview

Citizens broadly endorsed the need for AI regulation in high-stakes domains like hiring and lending, but a clear majority found the law insufficient - particularly the absence of explicit prohibition clauses that the EU AI Act includes. Even moderate-conservative participants who defended the law's framework acknowledged implementation gaps. Startup burden was recognized but rarely prioritized over citizen protection.

3. Key Themes

  • Prohibition gap vs. EU benchmark: Most participants contrasted Korea's approach unfavorably with the EU's bans on social scoring and real-time biometric surveillance. Yang Heejin: "EU는 아예 금지했는데, 한국은 그냥 '안전성 평가'로 퉁치는 거잖아요."
  • Algorithmic discrimination in hiring/lending: Multiple citizens raised concerns about AI filtering out vulnerable populations - career-interrupted women, freelancers, union members. Nam Gihoon: "노조 활동 이력이나 산재 경력 있는 사람 걸러내는 거 시간문제 아닙니까."
  • Implementation vacuum: Ryu Junhyeok noted that enforcement personnel and evaluation infrastructure budgets remain unresolved - "법부터 시행하는 느낌이 강합니다."
  • Startup survival vs. citizen protection trade-off: Kim Minjun warned compliance costs could consume 20-30% of small firms' revenue; progressives countered that firms unable to afford compliance shouldn't operate high-risk AI.

4. Demographic Patterns

Progressives (Lee, Im, Nam, Choi) uniformly demanded prohibition clauses and centered vulnerable populations. Moderate-conservatives (Kang, Kim, Oh) defended incremental regulation and prioritized industrial competitiveness. Age mattered less than political leaning, though older progressives (Choi, 67) framed the issue through democratic history. Seoul/Gyeonggi residents focused on algorithmic discrimination; Sejong's Ryu offered rare insider bureaucratic perspective.

5. Communication Gaps

Citizens lacked awareness that the EU AI Act's prohibitions only took effect in phases (February 2025 onward) with limited enforcement data available yet. Moderator Report

Yeouido Agora Discussion Report

1. Stimulus

The 22nd National Assembly has processed zero livelihood bills amid the impeachment crisis, with plenary sessions suspended for a full month due to prolonged partisan gridlock.

2. Sentiment Overview

Citizen reaction was uniformly angry across all political leanings. The dominant emotion was not partisan blame but a shared sense of betrayal - legislators collect salaries while ordinary people bear the cost of inaction. Even conservatives critical of the opposition framed their frustration primarily in pocketbook terms, not ideological ones.

3. Key Themes

Legislative dereliction as wage theft. Multiple citizens invoked the salary comparison: "Their pay arrives on time; our relief doesn't." Kim Minjun calculated ~₩4 billion wasted monthly. Nam Gihoon noted, "We lose a day's wage if we skip work; they lose nothing for skipping a month."

Livelihood as hostage of political conflict. Yang Heejin's cross-national comparison - Japan and Taiwan processed economic legislation during their own crises - crystallized the structural critique. Choi Youngho reinforced: "The National Assembly Act provides procedures; what's missing is will, not method."

Disproportionate harm to the vulnerable. Im Hajin and Ahn Suji argued the burden falls heaviest on precarious workers, single parents, and caregivers awaiting stalled childcare and emergency-care bills.

Bipartisan blame with asymmetric attribution. While most citizens faulted both parties, conservatives (Park Sunhee, Kwon Taesung) specifically blamed the opposition for weaponizing impeachment. Progressives distributed blame more evenly.

4. Demographic Patterns

Age mattered more than region. Younger citizens (30s) demanded data-driven accountability; older conservatives (50s-70s) drew on historical memory and personal business hardship. Gyeonggi mothers (Jung, Hwang, Ahn) converged on childcare/cost-of-living concerns regardless of stated ideology, suggesting caregiving burdens cut across partisan lines.

5. Communication Gaps

Citizens largely did not distinguish between bills blocked by plenary suspension versus those stalled in committee. Several assumed all pending legislation requires plenary action, underestimating executive-order or committee-level workarounds. Kwon Taesung's nostalgia for authoritarian-era efficiency conflates rubber-stamp legislatures with genuine legislative productivity.

6. Top Research Demands

  1. Class-disaggregated cost of legislative paralysis: Quantify which income quintiles and employment types bear the greatest policy loss when bills stall (Im Hajin).
  2. Economic damage metrics for self-employed and workers: Measure how parliamentary shutdowns correlate with small-business closure rates, industrial accident outcomes, and childcare cost increases (Park Sunhee, Nam Gihoon, Ahn Suji).
  3. Cross-national institutional safeguards: How do other democracies insulate livelihood legislation from political crises? (Kwon Taesung, Yang Heejin).

7. Suggested Research Topic

--topic "Institutional mechanisms for insulating livelihood legislation from political crises: cross-national evidence from OECD democracies and distributional consequences of legislative paralysis by income and employment type"
