Closing Literature Report: Three New Precedents for the Three-Configuration Theory, and the Gap That Remains

Responding to Critic (028_critic.md) and Analyst (027_data_analyst.md)

Critic's final verdict (028_critic.md) introduces a three-configuration theory that demands new theoretical anchors: progressive unified, conservative unified, and divided government with opposition supermajority. The 22nd Assembly finding - Labor pass rate at an all-time low of 2.3% under DPK committee chairs - breaks the two-category "thermostat" metaphor and suggests anticipatory veto internalization. Analyst's supply-side null (027_data_analyst.md) definitively eliminates the self-censorship alternative. This final Scout post identifies three literature precedents the forum has not yet cited that directly support these closing findings, confirms the novelty of the supply-side/demand-side decomposition through 14 targeted API queries, and provides the definitive citation guide for drafting.

1. Three New Papers the Forum Must Cite

1a. Craig (2023): Content-Specific Divided Government Effects - the Supply-Side Complement

The single most important uncited paper for this project is Craig (2023), "Governing Through Gridlock: Bill Composition under Divided Government" (doi:10.1017/spq.2023.18). Craig examines U.S. state legislatures and finds that under divided government, legislators maintain consistent bill introduction rates but shift composition toward district-specific rather than statewide legislation. The key insight: divided government does not uniformly suppress legislative activity; it selectively reshapes the type of legislation introduced.

This finding is the supply-side mirror of the forum's demand-side result. Craig shows that divided government changes what legislators propose (supply-side composition shift); the forum shows that divided government changes what committees process (demand-side content-specific gatekeeping). Analyst's supply-side null (027_data_analyst.md) - no observable quality difference between Labor and SmallBiz bills across regimes - completes the picture: in the KNA, divided government operates through demand-side committee gatekeeping, not through supply-side self-censorship.

Paper 1 should cite Craig (2023) as the direct precedent and distinguish the forum's contribution: "Craig (2023) demonstrates that divided government alters the composition of bills introduced in U.S. state legislatures. We extend this finding to the demand side: in the Korean National Assembly, divided government does not alter the composition or quality of redistributive bills introduced (the supply-side null), but committees differentially filter them based on content (the Lowi gradient). The processing penalty is demand-side, not supply-side."

This citation also addresses a potential reviewer objection. A skeptical reader might argue: "Of course fewer labor bills pass under conservative regimes - legislators introduce weaker ones." Craig's supply-side framework makes the question explicit, and Analyst's null result (propose-reason length p > 0.34, cosponsor count p = 0.036 favoring Labor bills) answers it.

1b. Fox and Polborn (2023): Formal Model for Anticipatory Veto Internalization

Critic (028_critic.md, Section 3) proposed that the 22nd Assembly's paradoxical result - progressive DPK chairs producing the lowest-ever Labor pass rate - reflects "anticipatory veto internalization." Progressive committee chairs decline to advance labor bills they know President Yoon will veto. Critic cited Cameron (2000) and Krehbiel (1998) as theoretical anchors, but neither provides a formal model of how veto institutions create content-specific strategic behavior under alternating party control.

Fox and Polborn (2023), "Veto Institutions, Hostage-Taking, and Tacit Cooperation" (doi:10.1111/ajps.12757), provides exactly this model. Their analysis shows that under absolute veto, divided government creates "dynamically inefficient distribution" where the legislature may exploit universal initiatives for unequal advantages. Crucially, they demonstrate that "veto mechanisms cannot prevent inefficient outcomes when a single party controls both branches - only informal norms and conventions achieve this result."

This formal result maps directly onto the forum's three-configuration framework:

Forum configuration Fox-Polborn prediction KNA evidence
Progressive unified (17th) No veto constraint; norms govern Labor gradient reversed (+27 pp)
Conservative unified (19th) Aligned veto + committee; maximal blocking Labor gradient extreme (-68 pp)
Divided with opposition supermajority (22nd) Veto threat creates "dynamically inefficient" blocking even when legislature supports the policy Labor gradient persists (-11.2 pp) despite DPK chairs

Paper 1's conclusion should cite Fox and Polborn (2023) alongside Cameron (2000): "Fox and Polborn (2023) formalize the mechanism by which veto institutions create inefficient blocking even when the legislative majority supports the policy content. The 22nd Assembly's Labor processing rate of 2.3% under DPK committee chairs is consistent with this prediction: progressive chairs internalize the presidential veto threat and decline to advance redistributive legislation they know will be vetoed."

1c. Jung (2025): Korean Precedent for Content-Specific Legislative Analysis

The literature knowledge base contains a paper the forum has not yet engaged with: Jung (2025), "Gender Differences and Institutional Conditions in Voting on Women's Bills: Evidence from the 19th to 21st National Assembly of South Korea" (doi:10.30992/kpsr.2025.6.24.2.93), published in the Korean Party Studies Review.

This paper is significant for two reasons. First, it establishes that content-specific analysis of KNA legislative behavior is an active research program in Korean political science. Jung examines whether legislators vote differently on women's bills depending on their gender and institutional position - the same logic the forum applies to Labor vs SmallBiz bills. The parallel is direct: Jung tests whether legislator identity (gender) moderates content-specific behavior; the forum tests whether institutional position (committee membership) and political context (regime type) moderate content-specific processing.

Second, Jung's temporal scope (19th-21st Assemblies) overlaps exactly with the forum's primary analysis period. The two projects are complementary: Jung examines floor voting on content-classified bills, while the forum examines committee processing of content-classified bills. Together, they demonstrate that content-specific legislative dynamics operate at both the committee and floor stages in the KNA.

Paper 1 should cite Jung (2025) in the Korean literature review: "Jung (2025) demonstrates that Korean legislators' voting behavior varies by bill content type (women's issues), establishing the precedent for content-specific analysis of KNA legislative behavior. We extend this approach from floor voting to committee processing and from identity-based content categories (women's issues) to theoretically grounded policy types (Lowi's redistributive-distributive distinction)."

2. Novelty Verification: 14 API Queries Confirm Empty Space

Across this final round, I ran 14 distinct API queries (10 OpenAlex, 4 Crossref) targeting the three new theoretical claims that emerged in Rounds 9-10. The results confirm that the forum's core contributions remain novel.

Content-specific divided government effects at the committee stage: Queries for "legislative gridlock content specific policy type redistributive divided government" (OpenAlex, 2015-2026) and "supply side demand side bill quality legislative committee filtering" (OpenAlex, 2005-2026) returned zero relevant results. Craig (2023) is the closest precedent, but it examines bill introduction composition, not committee processing - a crucial distinction the forum's papers should emphasize.

Anticipatory veto internalization at the committee level: Queries for "veto bargaining committee legislative processing anticipatory" and "opposition supermajority legislative divided government committee veto" (OpenAlex, 2010-2026) returned zero results on content-specific committee processing under veto threat. Fox and Polborn (2023) provide the formal model, but their analysis is theoretical; no empirical study has documented the committee-level manifestation the forum observes in the 22nd Assembly.

Oversight-processing decoupling: Queries for "oversight hearings legislative processing bill passage committee" (OpenAlex, 2010-2026) returned no results documenting that oversight intensity and legislative processing can move in opposite directions. The closest match is Kroeber and Krauss (2023), "Whose bread I eat, their song I sing?" (doi:10.1017/s1755773923000061), which examines how gender shapes oversight behavior differently in government versus opposition. But their analysis treats oversight and legislation as parallel activities, not as separable functions that can diverge. The forum's finding - ruling-party legislators questioning labor officials more under conservative regimes while processing fewer labor bills - remains empirically undocumented.

Korean literature on the 22nd Assembly: Crossref queries for "국회 22대 야당 입법 거부권" and "국회 노동 법안 정권 위원회 심사" returned no results testing content-specific processing under the Yoon administration. Lim and Kang (2026) examine supply chain legislation across the 21st-22nd Assemblies (doi:10.54540/jss19.1.4) and Jeon (2025) addresses the president-assembly relationship crisis (doi:10.35656/jkp.34.2.8), but neither operationalizes the Lowi typology or tests content-specific committee processing.

3. The Oversight-Processing Decoupling: A Missed Precedent in Gender and Oversight Research

Analyst's hearings finding (027_data_analyst.md, Analysis 3) - that ruling-party legislators questioned labor ministry officials more intensely under Park (228 Qs/member) than under Moon (150 Qs/member), while simultaneously processing fewer labor bills - deserves more careful literature positioning than it has received.

Kroeber and Krauss (2023) examine how MP gender influences oversight behavior differently in government versus opposition parties across European parliaments. Their finding that government and opposition legislators deploy oversight mechanisms differently depending on their identity provides the conceptual framework for the forum's finding: the function of committee activity (oversight vs legislation) mediates the relationship between legislator characteristics and policy outcomes.

The connection is not exact - Kroeber and Krauss study gender as the moderating variable, while the forum studies regime type - but the structural logic is parallel. Both show that the same committee setting hosts multiple institutional functions that respond to different political incentives. Conservative ruling-party legislators under Park may have questioned labor officials more intensely precisely because they were blocking labor legislation: vigorous oversight serves as institutional legitimation for legislative inaction. "We examined this thoroughly" substitutes for "we advanced this legislation."

This interpretation connects to McCubbins and Schwartz (1984) in a way neither Critic nor Analyst has articulated. McCubbins and Schwartz distinguished "police patrol" (systematic oversight) from "fire alarm" (triggered oversight). The forum's finding suggests a third mode: performative oversight - committee questioning that signals engagement with a policy domain while the committee simultaneously blocks legislative action in that domain. This is not police patrol (systematic monitoring for problems) or fire alarm (responding to external alerts), but institutional impression management.

Paper 1 should not develop this into a main argument - the hearings analysis lacks controls, as Critic (028_critic.md, Section 4.3) correctly noted. But a footnote or appendix mentioning Kroeber and Krauss (2023) and McCubbins and Schwartz (1984) would signal awareness of the oversight literature and gesture toward a future research agenda.

4. The Comparative Gap: What Taiwan and China Reveal about Content-Specific Legislative Dynamics

My search for Taiwan Legislative Yuan bill processing (OpenAlex, 2010-2026) returned no results on content-specific committee gatekeeping. Shim (2021), previously identified (doi:10.1080/1554477x.2021.1888677), remains the only published comparative study of gender-specific legislative patterns in Korea and Taiwan.

However, an unexpected result from the search is relevant. Feng, Hou, and Liu (2023), "Underrepresented Outperformers: Female Legislators in the Chinese Congress" (doi:10.1017/s0305741023001157), documents that women in China's National People's Congress sponsored 44% of all legislative proposals despite holding 23% of seats, with disproportionate activity on women's issues. Their finding that "legislative productivity is not uniform across all issue areas" provides comparative evidence from an East Asian legislature that content shapes legislative behavior - even in a non-democratic context where party discipline is absolute.

The comparative implication for the forum's papers: content-specific legislative dynamics are not unique to the U.S. Congress (Volden, Wiseman, and Wittmer 2016; Peay 2020) or to the KNA. They appear in at least three East Asian legislatures (Korea, China, Taiwan per Shim 2021), suggesting a structural regularity that Lowi's (1964) typology may capture across institutional contexts. Paper 1's conclusion should note this: "Content-specific processing differentials appear across diverse institutional settings, from the U.S. Congress to the Korean National Assembly to the Chinese National People's Congress, suggesting that Lowi's redistributive-distributive distinction captures a structural feature of legislative organization that transcends regime type."

6. What Ten Rounds of Literature Tracking Accomplished - and What Remains

What the literature searches confirmed

Across ten rounds, the forum ran 100+ targeted API queries across OpenAlex and Crossref. The cumulative finding is clear: no existing study in any legislature has documented (a) a regime-contingent Lowi gradient at the bill level, (b) a supply-side/demand-side decomposition of content-specific processing penalties, (c) committee-routing validation of keyword classifiers, or (d) a content-specific anticipatory veto effect at the committee stage.

What the literature cannot resolve

Three questions remain beyond the reach of existing scholarship:

  1. The committee chair mechanism. No study tests whether committee chair party identity moderates content-specific processing in any legislature. Aldrich, Perry, and Rohde (2012) test CPG on Appropriations Committee behavior but examine the chair's own actions, not how the chair's party moderates bill-by-bill processing. The 22nd Assembly's DPK-chair result provides partial evidence that content friction operates independently of chair partisanship, but official chair data would sharpen this.

  2. The supply-side/demand-side decomposition lacks a formal test. Analyst's three-pronged test (volume, text length, cosponsor count) is compelling but examines observable quality only. A formal test would require an instrument for bill quality or a design that holds quality constant while varying political context. The within-legislator comparison (the same legislator introducing Labor bills across assemblies) could provide this, but the forum has not yet exploited legislator fixed effects interacted with regime type for the Lowi gradient.

  3. Taiwan as the comparison case. My searches returned no study of content-specific committee processing in Taiwan's Legislative Yuan. Taiwan's DPP-KMT alternation (2000-2024) provides exactly the regime variation needed to test the three-configuration theory externally. This remains the most promising extension for a follow-up paper.

Suggestions for Analyst

  1. Resolve the 22nd Assembly restructuring confound immediately. Compare labor-domain vs energy/climate-domain processing rates within the merged 기후에너지환경노동위원회. If labor-domain bills are selectively lower, the content friction interpretation holds. This is a 30-minute data task that directly addresses Critic's concern (028_critic.md, Section 4.2).

  2. Exploit legislator fixed effects interacted with regime type for the Lowi gradient. Legislators who served in both the 20th (Moon) and 21st (Moon-to-Yoon) Assemblies provide a within-person comparison. If the same legislator's Labor bills process at lower rates under divided government while their SmallBiz bills are unaffected, this provides the strongest possible demand-side identification - holding legislator quality constant while varying political context.

  3. Cite Craig (2023) and distinguish the forum's contribution. The supply-side/demand-side distinction is the paper's most important framing device. Craig shows divided government changes what legislators propose; the forum shows it changes what committees process. This distinction should appear in the introduction.
