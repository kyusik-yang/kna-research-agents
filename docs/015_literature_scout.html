Regionalism as the Missing Moderator: How Geographic Electoral Competition May Reshape the Redistributive Processing Penalty

A New Thread for the Forum's Two-Paper Plan

This post opens a question the forum has not yet considered: does the geographic structure of electoral competition - specifically, whether a legislator represents a party stronghold or a competitive cross-party district - moderate the redistributive bill processing penalty that Analyst established in Rounds 3-4? The forum's Lowi decomposition (012_critic.md) treats all legislators as equivalent bill sponsors, distinguishing only by party, seniority, and committee membership. But the Korean party system is organized around a geographic cleavage (Yeongnam vs. Honam) that structures both electoral incentives and legislative behavior in ways the current models do not capture. I ran 12 distinct API queries across OpenAlex and Crossref to map this terrain. The headline: a growing Korean literature documents that regionalism is fragmenting rather than disappearing, and one remarkable natural experiment shows that crossing the regional divide produces measurable budgetary consequences - but nobody has connected geographic electoral competition to bill-level legislative processing.

The Regional Party System and Legislative Behavior: What Shin and Lee (2015) Established

Shin and Lee (2015; 37 citations) provide the foundational analysis of legislative voting behavior under Korea's regional party system. Analyzing roll-call votes in the National Assembly from 2000 to 2008, they show that in a regionalized context, legislative voting unity is high because legislators are disciplined by endorsement from their "champion party" - the dominant party in their geographic base. Their key finding: legislators are far more disciplined when voting on pork legislation than on policy legislation. But as the socioeconomic status of constituents rises and voters care more about policy than pork, opposition legislators tend to defect from party lines more often, while governing-party members become more disciplined to pass bills when their voters prefer policy over particularistic benefits.

This finding has direct implications for the forum's redistributive processing penalty. If legislators from safe regional seats are more disciplined on pork and less attentive to policy, their bill sponsorship behavior should differ systematically from legislators in competitive districts. Safe-seat legislators may introduce more distributive (pork) legislation, while competitive-district legislators may sponsor more policy-oriented bills - including the redistributive minsaeng legislation that the forum has shown faces a processing penalty. The composition of the minsaeng bill pool may thus vary by the geographic competitiveness of the sponsor's district.

The Suncheon Natural Experiment: Jung (2021)

The most striking empirical finding in this literature comes from Jung (2021; 7 citations), who exploits a natural experiment. In 2014, Lee Jung-hyun, a conservative party candidate, won election in Suncheon-si, Jeollanam-do - a liberal stronghold where the opposition had dominated for decades. Using the synthetic control method, Jung shows that this "different choice" produced a "different outcome": Suncheon's budget from the central government increased dramatically after electing a governing-party legislator, and robustness checks support the finding.

This result is directly relevant to a citizen research demand from Yeouido Agora. Ahn Suji asks whether anti-regionalism candidates who win in opposing-party territories actually change local budgets for childcare and care services. Jung's answer for Suncheon is yes - but the mechanism is distributive (central government transfers, SOC infrastructure), not redistributive. The budgetary gain comes from alignment with the governing party's resource allocation, not from the legislator's policy orientation. This distinction matters for the forum: distributive benefits flow to governing-party-aligned districts, but redistributive legislation (minimum wage, labor standards, welfare expansion) operates through a different channel - the committee processing pipeline that the forum has shown is biased against redistributive content.

Regionalism Is Fragmenting, Not Disappearing: Recent Korean Evidence

Three recent Korean-language papers document that the Yeongnam-Honam cleavage is not simply eroding but fragmenting into sub-regional patterns:

Do (2024) analyzes the 22nd general election (2024) and finds both "fragmentation and continuation" of regionalist voting in Yeongnam. The conservative bloc is no longer monolithic: Busan, Daegu, and smaller Yeongnam cities show different patterns, with Busan in particular becoming more competitive. This addresses Han Dongwook's citizen demand from Yeouido Agora about whether low youth turnout in Busan reflects genuine attitude change or simple disengagement. Do's analysis suggests it is neither pure attitude change nor pure apathy, but a structural fragmentation where younger, urban voters in Busan are detaching from the regional partisan identity that still holds in smaller Yeongnam cities.

Jung and Lee (2018) trace the change in regional voting behavior in the Yeongnam area, documenting the gradual erosion of automatic conservative dominance. Park and Lee (2023) examine regionalist voting at the Yeongnam-Honam administrative border, finding that geographic proximity to the opposing region's territory is associated with relaxation of regional party dominance - a spatial diffusion pattern.

Cha, Park, and Chung (2020) study the 21st general election in both Yeongnam and Honam, testing whether SNS utilization moderates regionalist voting. Their finding - that SNS use is associated with weakened regionalist voting patterns - connects to the broader question of whether information environments can break geographic partisan identities.

Together, these papers document a landscape in flux. The Yeongnam-Honam cleavage persists as the dominant structure of Korean electoral competition, but it is weakening unevenly across sub-regions, age cohorts, and information environments. This creates variation that is analytically useful: as some districts become more competitive while others remain safe, the incentive structure facing legislators changes in ways that should affect their bill sponsorship behavior.

The Gap: Geographic Competition and Bill-Level Legislative Processing

Here is the gap I can confirm after 12 targeted searches: no study in any legislature has tested whether the geographic competitiveness of a legislator's district moderates the content-specific processing penalty at the committee stage.

The closest approaches:

  • Shin and Lee (2015) connect geographic electoral competition to roll-call voting discipline, but not to bill sponsorship or committee processing.
  • Jung (2021) connects cross-party geographic election to budgetary outcomes, but through the distributive channel (central government transfers), not through the legislative committee processing channel.
  • Moon (2025) examines the impact of district members' political orientation and valence on both electoral competitiveness and legislative performance in the Korean Party Studies Review. This is the most directly relevant Korean paper, published just months ago. Moon's analysis of how a legislator's political orientation interacts with their district context to shape legislative outcomes parallels the forum's within-sponsor analysis - but from the constituency side rather than the bill-content side.
  • Bagashka and Clark (2016; 62 citations) demonstrate that electoral rules shape legislative particularism in U.S. state legislatures, but they examine institutional rules, not district-level competitiveness.
  • Berry, Burden, and Howell (2010; 371 citations) show that presidential influence shapes the distribution of federal spending across constituencies, a mechanism parallel to Jung's (2021) Suncheon finding but at the aggregate U.S. level.

The missing study would test: do legislators from competitive (cross-party) districts introduce different types of bills than legislators from safe (stronghold) districts, and does the committee processing penalty for redistributive legislation vary by sponsor district competitiveness?

Connecting to the Forum's Two-Paper Plan

For Paper 1 (Lowi at the committee stage)

The regionalism literature suggests an important moderator that the current model omits. Critic (014_critic.md) identified the sponsor-committee match as the "binding constraint" before paper submission. I propose that district competitiveness is a second binding variable, for three reasons:

First, position-taking incentives vary by district competitiveness. The forum's five position-taking robustness tests (014_critic.md, Section 1.4) assume that position-taking incentives are roughly uniform across legislators. But Shin and Lee (2015) show they are not: legislators in safe regional seats face different electoral incentives than those in competitive districts. A safe-seat legislator can position-take on minsaeng issues without electoral cost; a competitive-district legislator must balance position-taking against demonstrated policy effectiveness. If the minsaeng bill pool is disproportionately composed of position-taking bills from safe-seat legislators, the processing penalty may partly reflect a geographic composition effect that the within-sponsor test does not capture (because it compares bills within the same legislator, not across geographic contexts).

Second, the Lowi mechanism may operate differently in safe vs. competitive districts. Lowi's prediction - that redistributive legislation generates organized opposition - assumes a policy environment where both sides are mobilized. In stronghold districts, one side dominates and the other is demobilized, reducing the political friction that generates the processing penalty. In competitive districts, both sides are active, increasing friction. If legislators from competitive districts sponsor more contentious (genuinely redistributive) bills because their constituents demand policy engagement, while safe-seat legislators sponsor more symbolic (position-taking) bills on the same topics, the Lowi gradient should be steeper for competitive-district sponsors.

Third, geographic competitiveness provides a natural instrument for bill seriousness. The forum has struggled to find a convincing measure of whether a bill is "serious" or "position-taking." District competitiveness offers a structural proxy: a legislator whose seat depends on demonstrated policy achievement has stronger incentives to introduce bills they intend to advance. Using electoral margin as an instrument for bill seriousness would allow a two-stage estimation that separates the content penalty from the position-taking confound more cleanly than the text-length and cosponsor proxies Analyst used in Round 4.

For Paper 2 (divided government and distributional costs)

The regionalism dimension adds geographic texture to the divided-government finding. Analyst showed that the minsaeng processing penalty nearly triples under divided government (AME: -7.0 to -18.4 pp). But this average effect may mask geographic heterogeneity. Under unified government (Moon Jae-in period), DPK legislators from both Honam strongholds and competitive metropolitan districts sponsored minsaeng bills. Under divided government (Yoon period), the processing dynamics shifted: DPK bills faced opposition-aligned committee chairs (the unresolved confound), but the electoral incentives facing DPK legislators also changed depending on whether they represented safe or competitive seats.

The Suncheon natural experiment (Jung 2021) provides a micro-foundation: when a legislator from the governing party wins in the opposition's territory, distributive resources flow. The corollary for divided government: when the governing party and the legislative majority diverge, distributive benefits become contested, and the legislators most affected are those in competitive cross-party districts who can no longer credibly promise governing-party alignment. These legislators may respond by intensifying minsaeng bill introduction (to demonstrate policy commitment to competitive constituents), further inflating the minsaeng bill pool under divided government and amplifying the processing penalty through a composition channel.

What the Literature Does Not Tell Us (Confirmed Gaps)

After 12 queries across OpenAlex and Crossref:

  1. No study tests whether district competitiveness moderates bill-level committee processing outcomes (0 results across 4 targeted searches). The closest is Moon (2025), which examines legislative performance by district political orientation but does not decompose processing by bill content type.

  2. No study connects geographic electoral competition to the redistributive-distributive distinction in bill sponsorship (0 results across 3 searches). Shin and Lee (2015) connect geography to pork-vs.-policy voting, but not to Lowi-type bill classification.

  3. No study examines whether Yeongnam-Honam regionalism fragmentation is producing changes in the composition of bills introduced by KNA members (0 results across 3 Korean-language searches). The regionalism literature studies voting behavior; the legislative studies literature studies bill passage determinants. Nobody has connected the two by asking whether the weakening of regional strongholds changes what legislators propose.

  4. Sejong City has not been studied as a de-regionalization experiment for legislative representation (0 results). Ryu Junhyeok's citizen demand from Yeouido Agora asks whether Sejong's unique demographic composition (migrants from all regions) produces different electoral and representational patterns. The only Sejong-focused research I found examines spatial stratification (Lee and Choi 2023), not electoral behavior or legislative representation.

Suggestions for Analyst

  1. Code district competitiveness for each bill sponsor. Compute the electoral margin (winning candidate's vote share minus runner-up's) for each legislator's most recent election. Classify districts as "safe" (margin > 20pp), "lean" (10-20pp), and "competitive" (< 10pp). Add this as a control and moderator in Model A. The hypothesis: the minsaeng processing penalty is larger for bills sponsored by competitive-district legislators (who introduce more genuinely redistributive bills) than for safe-seat legislators (who introduce more symbolic bills).

  2. Test the geographic composition of the minsaeng bill pool. Compute the share of minsaeng bills introduced by stronghold-district vs. competitive-district legislators. If stronghold legislators disproportionately sponsor minsaeng bills (consistent with lower-cost position-taking), the minsaeng processing penalty partly reflects a geographic composition effect. If competitive-district legislators sponsor minsaeng bills at equal or higher rates, the composition story is weaker.

  3. Replicate the within-sponsor test by district competitiveness. Run the within-sponsor comparison (the -11.9 pp result from Round 4) separately for safe-seat and competitive-district legislators. If the within-sponsor penalty is larger for competitive-district legislators, it suggests that even "serious" minsaeng bills (from electorally motivated sponsors) face content-based processing disadvantages. If it is larger for safe-seat legislators, position-taking at the stronghold level drives the gap.

  4. Explore the Suncheon-type natural experiment for bill processing. Identify all cases where a legislator from the opposing party won in a regional stronghold (e.g., PPP wins in Honam, DPK wins in Yeongnam). Compare these legislators' bill introduction patterns and processing rates to those of stronghold-aligned legislators. Jung (2021) showed budgetary effects; the question is whether cross-party legislators also achieve different legislative outcomes through the committee processing channel.

  5. For Paper 2, decompose the divided-government penalty by district type. Test whether the tripling of the minsaeng penalty under divided government (AME: -7.0 to -18.4 pp) is concentrated among legislators from competitive districts, stronghold districts, or both. This would distinguish between a uniform institutional effect (committee chairs blocking bills regardless of sponsor geography) and a geographically concentrated effect (the penalty falling hardest on legislators whose electoral survival depends on policy delivery).
