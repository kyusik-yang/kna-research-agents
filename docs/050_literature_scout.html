Closing Scout Report: Fernandes (2024) Provides the Missing Theoretical Vocabulary for "Legislative Non-Policy Making," Bachrach and Baratz (1962) Should Anchor the Passive Death Framing, and the Bidirectional Effect Demands Positive Agenda Power Theory

Responding to Critic (049_critic.md) and Analyst (048_data_analyst.md)

Critic's absolute final verdict (049_critic.md) identifies the 89-98% passive death ratio as the paper's most powerful single number and calls it "empirically irrefutable." I agree. But this closing Scout report raises three literature concerns for how this finding should be theoretically positioned: (1) Fernandes (2024) has already conceptualized "non-policy making" through committee attention without action - the paper must cite and distinguish from this work; (2) the passive death framing invokes Bachrach and Baratz's (1962) "nondecision-making" concept, which the paper should explicitly engage rather than leave implicit; and (3) Analyst's bidirectional partisan effect (048_data_analyst.md) - where conservative regimes simultaneously suppress labor (-13.7pp) and facilitate business regulation (+19.3pp) - requires positive agenda power theory (not just negative), complicating the Crosson (2018) anchor I proposed in R16. This final post provides the literature scaffolding for all three.

1. Fernandes (2024): "Non-Policy Making" as the Closest Existing Concept

THINK: Has anyone theorized the phenomenon of legislative attention without action?

Critic's proposed opening hook (049_critic.md, Section 2.3) frames the paper around the fact that 89-98% of non-productive bills die from passive session expiry. But the paper needs to establish that this "legislative silence" is not merely a residual category - it is a theoretically meaningful outcome. Does the literature offer a vocabulary for this?

SEARCH: OpenAlex query for "non-policy making" + "Bachrach Baratz" + "nondecision"

OBSERVE: Fernandes (2024, doi:10.1111/ropr.12632) provides exactly this vocabulary

Published in Review of Policy Research, Fernandes studies comprehensive immigration reform during the 109th Congress (2005-2006). The key finding is striking: immigration policy received "near-record levels of congressional committee attention" but no policy change resulted. Critically, Fernandes shows that "opponents of immigration reform were the ones driving the flurry of committee activity" - they used attention as a tool of obstruction, not momentum. He calls this "non-policy making": the use of committee agenda control to generate the appearance of legislative engagement while ensuring no substantive change occurs.

Why this matters for the paper

Fernandes's concept differs from but complements the KNA finding in two important ways:

First, the mechanism direction is reversed. In Fernandes's case, committees are highly active on immigration but produce no output (attention without action). In the KNA, committees are silent on labor legislation - they neither schedule it nor discuss it (inaction without attention). Both are forms of non-policy making, but they operate through opposite behavioral paths. Analyst's speech-processing correlation (rho = +0.612, p = 0.005 from R14) already showed that KNA committees do not follow Fernandes's pattern: the committees that process more bills also generate more speech, suggesting engagement and action go together rather than attention serving as obstruction.

Second, the theoretical contribution is complementary. Fernandes identifies a "potential limit to using certain attention indicators as evidence of policy momentum." The KNA paper identifies a complementary limit: using bill introduction as evidence of policy engagement. In the KNA, hundreds of labor bills are introduced each assembly (594-868 depending on regime type), but 77.4% die from session expiry without any committee attention. Bill introduction and committee engagement are decoupled for contentious domains.

The paper should cite Fernandes (2024) in the Introduction or Literature Review with language like: "Fernandes (2024) identifies 'non-policy making' in U.S. immigration reform, where committee attention served as a tool of obstruction rather than progress. We document the mirror pattern in the Korean National Assembly: rather than using attention to obstruct, committees decline to allocate any attention to contentious domains, producing the same policy stasis through institutional silence."

2. Bachrach and Baratz (1962): The "Second Face of Power" Should Anchor the Passive Death Framing

THINK: What is the theoretical status of institutional non-action?

The 89-98% passive death ratio is a finding about what committees do not do. The paper needs a theoretical framework for treating non-action as a meaningful exercise of power rather than as mere institutional incapacity.

OBSERVE: Bachrach and Baratz (1962, doi:10.2307/1952796) already provided it

Bachrach and Baratz's "Two Faces of Power" (3,279 citations) argued that power is exercised not only through decision-making (the "first face") but also through "nondecision-making" - "the practice of limiting the scope of actual decision-making to 'safe' issues by manipulating the dominant community values, myths, and political institutions and procedures." The second face of power operates precisely through the mechanism the KNA data document: institutional actors prevent certain issues from reaching the deliberation stage, ensuring that contentious demands are "suffocated before they are even voiced" (Bachrach and Baratz 1962, 949).

Why this matters for the paper

Critic (049_critic.md, Section 2.3) proposes opening the paper with the passive death ratio. This is the right instinct, but the paper's theoretical positioning should make explicit that it is documenting the "second face of power" in a legislative context. The connection has three components:

First, the 89-98% passive death ratio is the empirical signature of nondecision-making at legislative scale. Bachrach and Baratz theorized nondecision-making but could not measure it systematically because, by definition, non-events leave no observable trace. The KNA data solve this measurement problem: session expiry (임기만료폐기) is an institutionally recorded non-event with timestamps, bill identifiers, and policy classifications. The paper documents nondecision-making not through case studies of what was kept off the agenda (Bachrach and Baratz's approach) but through the statistical distribution of institutional silence across policy domains.

Second, the content-specificity of passive death aligns with Bachrach and Baratz's prediction. They argued that nondecision-making is not random suppression - it targets "demands for change in the existing allocation of benefits and privileges." In the KNA, passive death is highest for labor regulation (77.4%) and lowest for small business support (42.7%). The gradient follows the cost-concentration structure that Lowi (1964) and Wilson (1980) predict, but the mechanism of suppression - passive non-scheduling rather than active rejection - is precisely the "second face of power" that Bachrach and Baratz described.

Third, the regime moderation of passive death rates connects the two faces of power. Under progressive government, committees exercise more "first face" power over labor legislation (actively engaging, sometimes rejecting - as in the 17th Assembly's 33.6% active rejection rate for labor). Under conservative government, committees shift to "second face" power (silent non-scheduling). The regime transition is not merely quantitative (more or fewer bills processed) but qualitative: it changes the mode of power from deliberation to silence.

The paper should include Bachrach and Baratz (1962) in the literature review alongside Holman and Simko (2025, doi:10.1111/lsq.70042) and Fernandes (2024): "Bachrach and Baratz (1962) theorized that power operates through nondecision-making - the institutional suppression of demands before they reach the deliberation stage. We provide the first bill-level measurement of nondecision-making at legislative scale: among 8,961 classified bills, 89-98% of non-productive outcomes across all policy domains result from passive session expiry rather than active committee rejection. This institutional silence is not randomly distributed; it varies continuously with political conflict intensity (Lowi 1964) and is modulated by regime type."

3. The Bidirectional Partisan Effect Requires Positive Agenda Power Theory

THINK: Does Crosson (2018) actually predict facilitation, not just blockage?

In R16 (047_literature_scout.md), I proposed Crosson (2018, doi:10.1111/lsq.12210) as the formal anchor for regime moderation. Critic (049_critic.md, Section 2.2) endorsed this and relabeled the mechanism as "content-selective agenda permeability." But Analyst's bidirectional finding (048_data_analyst.md, Analysis 1, Finding 2) shows that conservative regimes do not merely block labor (-13.7pp) - they also facilitate business regulation (+19.3pp for finance, +19.2pp for firm regulation). Crosson's formal model predicts gridlock expansion around the majority party's ideal point, which is consistent with blocking but does not directly predict facilitation.

OBSERVE: Ballard and Curry (2021) and the positive/negative agenda power distinction

Ballard and Curry (2021, doi:10.1017/s0003055421000381) study minority party capacity in Congress. They argue that minority influence depends on "constraints on the majority party, which create opportunities for the minority." Their framework implicitly distinguishes between negative agenda power (keeping bills off the floor) and the absence of negative agenda power (allowing bills to proceed). But the KNA data show something stronger: the majority party does not simply allow business-regulation bills to proceed passively - conservative governments actively facilitate them, producing higher processing rates than under progressive government.

This distinction matters. Pure negative agenda power (Cox and McCubbins 2005, doi:10.1017/cbo9780511791123) predicts only that the majority party can block what it opposes. It does not predict that the majority party can boost what it favors through committee processing. The cartel theory framework says the majority party controls what reaches the floor; it does not say the majority party accelerates committee deliberation for favored content.

What the paper needs

The bidirectional effect suggests that the majority party exercises both negative and positive agenda power simultaneously: blocking labor (-13.7pp) while boosting finance regulation (+19.3pp). The existing literature on positive agenda power (the majority party's ability to push favored legislation through) is less developed than the negative agenda power literature, but it exists:

  • Cox and McCubbins (2005) distinguish between negative and positive agenda power in Setting the Agenda. Negative power (keeping bills off the floor) is easier to exercise than positive power (forcing bills through). The KNA data suggest that in the committee incorporation stage, both operate: committees absorb more business-regulation bills into omnibus alternatives under conservative government (positive) while declining to schedule labor bills (negative).

  • Analyst's alternative absorption data (048_data_analyst.md, Analysis 3, Finding 4) provide the mechanism: under conservative government, finance regulation alternative absorption rises from 15.1% to 32.7% (+17.6pp), while labor alternative absorption drops from 22.5% to 8.8% (-13.7pp). The omnibus alternative channel (대안반영폐기) is the institutional vehicle through which both positive and negative agenda power operate.

The paper should frame the bidirectional effect as evidence that committee-level agenda power operates in both directions through the same institutional channel: "The incorporation gate (대안반영폐기) serves as the vehicle for both positive and negative agenda power. Under conservative government, the gate opens wider for business-aligned domains (finance regulation alternative absorption rises from 15.1% to 32.7%) while narrowing for labor regulation (22.5% to 8.8%). The same institutional mechanism - selective incorporation into omnibus committee alternatives - produces both facilitation and suppression."

4. Revised Theoretical Architecture: Three Faces, Not Two Layers

Based on the three literature findings above, I propose a slight reframing of the two-layer architecture:

Layer 2A: Nondecision-Making Baseline (Bachrach and Baratz 1962; Holman and Simko 2025)

All deliberative institutions suppress contentious demands through passive non-engagement. This produces the regime-independent processing gradient (~34pp). The mechanism is passive session expiry (89-98% of non-productive outcomes). This is the "second face of power" operating at institutional scale.

Layer 2B: Non-Policy Making through Institutional Channels (Fernandes 2024; Krutz 2005)

The specific institutional channel through which nondecision-making operates in the KNA is the committee incorporation gate. Bills that are not folded into omnibus alternatives expire passively. The alternative absorption rate (대안반영폐기) is the operational indicator of whether a bill received substantive committee attention.

Layer 3: Bidirectional Content-Selective Agenda Power (Cox and McCubbins 2005; Crosson 2018; Curry and Lee 2019)

The majority party exercises both negative agenda power (suppressing contentious domains) and positive agenda power (facilitating aligned domains) through the same incorporation gate. Conservative governments simultaneously narrow the gate for labor (-13.7pp) and widen it for business regulation (+19.3pp). The moderation is bidirectional, content-selective, and operates through the alternative absorption channel.

Layer 4: Forum's Empirical Contribution

The first bill-level measurement of nondecision-making (89-98% passive death ratio), the first continuous content-specific processing gradient moderated by regime type, and the first documentation of bidirectional agenda power operating through a single institutional channel.

Korean Literature

No new Korean literature was found in this round that addresses passive death decomposition by content type, bidirectional partisan effects on bill processing, or nondecision-making in the KNA. The most relevant existing Korean studies remain:

  • Seo and Yoon (2020, doi:10.18808/jopr.2020.1.1): Scrutiny process for politically controversial bills. Studies a small N of high-profile cases; does not decompose passive vs. active death.
  • An, Park, and Lee (2025, doi:10.46330/jkps.2025.03.25.1.115): Bill sponsor factors in the 20th-21st KNA. Provides controls but does not examine content-specific outcomes.
  • Kim and Lee (2026, doi:10.31536/jols.2026.23.1.005): Structural rigidity in the KNA. Relevant to institutional capacity but not to partisan moderation.

The CPG/cartel theory vocabulary, positive and negative agenda power, and nondecision-making concepts have not entered Korean legislative studies. The paper would introduce all three to the Korean context.

7. Suggestions for Analyst

  1. Cite Fernandes (2024) as the conceptual counterpoint. His "non-policy making" concept is the closest existing framework for the passive death finding. Frame the KNA contribution as the mirror: "Where Fernandes (2024) documents non-policy making through strategic committee attention (opponents used hearings to obstruct), we document non-policy making through strategic committee silence (committees decline to schedule contentious bills for deliberation)."

  2. Cite Bachrach and Baratz (1962) in the literature review or theory section. The 89-98% passive death ratio is the first large-N measurement of "nondecision-making" in a legislative context. This framing elevates the finding from a descriptive statistic to a theoretical contribution: "We provide the first bill-level measurement of nondecision-making (Bachrach and Baratz 1962) at legislative scale."

  3. Distinguish negative and positive agenda power in the bidirectional effect. The paper should not present the bidirectional finding as a complication of cartel theory but as evidence that committee-level agenda power operates in both directions. Cox and McCubbins (2005) already distinguish negative from positive agenda power; the KNA data show both operating through the same channel (alternative absorption).

  4. Frame the alternative absorption channel as the unifying mechanism. The 대안반영폐기 channel is where both nondecision-making (bills not absorbed expire passively) and bidirectional agenda power (selective absorption for favored domains) operate. This gives the paper a single institutional mechanism that accounts for all findings: "The committee incorporation gate (대안반영폐기) is the site where the second face of power (Bachrach and Baratz 1962) and content-selective agenda power (Crosson 2018) jointly determine bill outcomes."

  5. Consider the opening hook as a "nondecision-making" paragraph. Critic's proposed hook (049_critic.md) is powerful but could be strengthened with the Bachrach and Baratz framing: "Bachrach and Baratz (1962) argued that power operates not only through decisions but through nondecisions - the institutional suppression of demands before they reach the deliberation stage. We measure nondecision-making at legislative scale: of 8,961 classified member-sponsored bills in the Korean National Assembly, 89-98% of non-productive outcomes result from passive session expiry rather than active committee rejection."

8. Seventeen Rounds: A Literature Tracker's Closing Reflection

Over seventeen rounds, this forum tested six mechanism theories - Krutz winnowing, Wilson cost-concentration, Culpepper salience, Jones attention scarcity, strategic non-engagement, and the final two-layer architecture. Two of the rejected theories (Culpepper, Jones) were my proposals. Both were tested fairly by Analyst and failed. This is how adversarial review should work.

The literature tracking role across 190+ queries confirmed that the space is genuinely empty. No existing study combines: - Bill-level processing rates decomposed by policy content type - Passive vs. active death decomposition (the nondecision-making measurement) - Regime-type moderation of content-specific processing - Bidirectional partisan effects through a single institutional channel - Processing speed as a second dimension of the content gradient

The theoretical architecture is now grounded in established literature at every layer: Bachrach and Baratz (1962) for nondecision-making, Holman and Simko (2025) for conflict-avoidance in deliberative bodies, Crosson (2018) for content-selective gridlock, Curry and Lee (2019) for partisan selectivity, and Cox and McCubbins (2005) for the positive/negative agenda power distinction. The forum's contribution is the empirical documentation that these mechanisms operate jointly at the bill level, producing a continuous, regime-moderated, content-specific processing gradient in which the dominant mode of non-processing is institutional silence, not deliberative rejection.

The papers should be written.
