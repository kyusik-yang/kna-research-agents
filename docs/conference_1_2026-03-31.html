KNA Research Agents Conference #1

Proceedings | 2026-03-31 | 20 Forum Rounds

Keynote: State of KNA Research

Twenty rounds ago, the forum opened with a simple empirical question: where do Korean bills actually die in the legislative pipeline? The literature offered surprisingly little guidance. No survival model for bill duration existed in Korean legislative studies, no quasi-experimental design had been applied to committee processing, and no Korean adaptation of Legislative Effectiveness Scores had been attempted (Round 1). The field had produced rich qualitative accounts of legislative paralysis - the familiar narrative of 식물국회 - but lacked the bill-level infrastructure to measure what paralysis actually looks like from the inside.

The forum set out to fill that gap, and the 20 rounds that followed traced an arc from descriptive accounting to theoretical architecture. The initial finding was stark: 79.9 percent of failed bills in the 20th-21st Assemblies expired after being placed on a standing committee agenda but never receiving a decision (Round 1). This was death by institutional silence, not legislative combat. The Legislation and Judiciary Committee, long identified as the National Assembly's principal bottleneck, accounted for just 0.3 percent of bill deaths. The chokepoint was the standing committee itself - or more precisely, the standing committee's refusal to act.

From this descriptive foundation, the forum pursued three interlocking questions. First, is committee inaction content-blind or content-selective? Rounds 3 through 7 established that bills addressing labor, welfare, and care populations face a systematic processing penalty that survives every control the forum could construct - committee fixed effects, within-sponsor comparisons, arrival timing, cosponsor counts, and sponsor seniority. The penalty is demand-side: committees differentially filter bills based on content, not supply-side differences in bill quality (Round 9).

Second, where in the committee pipeline does the penalty operate? Rounds 11 and 12 identified the incorporation gate - the point at which committees decide which member bills to consolidate into omnibus alternatives (대안반영폐기) - as the content-specific chokepoint. Once a bill enters the omnibus pipeline, it passes at 99.8 percent. The filtering happens upstream, at the moment of selection.

Third, is the penalty a structural constant or a regime-contingent phenomenon? Rounds 8 through 10 overturned the forum's own earlier claim of structural invariance, revealing that the content gradient swings dramatically across presidential regimes: Labor Standards Act amendments processed at 89.7 percent under Roh Moo-hyun and collapsed to 5.6 percent under Park Geun-hye (Round 8). The gradient is not driven by bill volume or attention scarcity (both theories were proposed and rejected in Rounds 14-15) but by what the forum ultimately termed "regime-moderated political tractability" - the degree to which a given policy domain permits committee-level resolution under prevailing political conditions.

What remains open is substantial. The committee chair partisan gatekeeping hypothesis was flagged in every round from 2 through 19 but never definitively tested for lack of official roster data. The regime-contingent framework rests on five assembly-level observations, yielding a permutation p-value of 0.10 - honest but underpowered. The keyword classifier that anchors the policy content variable achieved only 44.2 percent coverage before committee-routing validation amplified the gradient, confirming attenuation bias rather than inflation, but a hand-coded validation sample was never completed. And the mechanism by which omnibus alternatives dilute or preserve substantive provisions remains unmeasurable from current data. The forum identified the gate; what happens after bills pass through it is a question for the next twenty rounds.

Panel 1: Empirical Findings

The Committee Graveyard

The forum's empirical program began with the full universe of 89,228 bills spanning the 17th through 21st Assemblies (Round 20). The foundational measurement was the committee inaction rate: among 32,335 failed bills in the 20th-21st Assemblies, 79.9 percent died at the agenda-to-decision transition - placed on the standing committee agenda but never processed (Round 1). This rate widened monotonically from 43 percent in the 17th Assembly to 64 percent in the 21st as bill volume tripled (Round 1). The 대안반영폐기 (alternative-bill incorporation) mechanism does not inflate this graveyard count; when "broad success" is measured to include content absorbed into omnibus alternatives, the success rate runs roughly five times higher than conventional passage, but the committee inaction finding holds (Rounds 2, 20).

The depth of institutional silence proved more extreme than the initial funnel suggested. Across 49,074 passively dead member bills, 97.9 percent never received a committee processing date - zero trace of deliberation over the entire four-year assembly term (Round 17). The median duration of this silence is 782 days, or 2.1 years (Round 18). Bills do not die through active rejection; 89 to 98 percent expire passively through session termination (Round 16). The committee system receives these bills, acknowledges them, and then generates no further institutional action.

The Content Gradient

Committee inaction is not content-blind. Bills classified as 민생법안 (livelihood legislation) targeting workers, care recipients, and welfare populations face a 9.3 percentage-point processing penalty after controlling for committee fixed effects, arrival timing, cosponsor counts, and sponsor seniority (AME from nested logistic regression, p < 0.001; Round 4). Among 327 legislators who introduced at least five bills in each category, the within-legislator gap is 11.9 percentage points (t = -10.06, p < 0.001), eliminating position-taking as an explanation (Round 4). Five independent tests - including proportional representatives whose labor bills have the lowest decision rate in the entire dataset despite lacking district-level electoral incentives - confirmed the penalty is demand-side (Rounds 5-6).

The gradient is continuous, not binary. Seven policy sub-categories form an ordered sequence from 17.1 percent (labor) to 49.5 percent (small business), with no clean break separating "concentrated-cost" from "diffuse-benefit" categories (Round 13). Veterans bills process like redistributive legislation despite being formally distributive, confirming political conflict intensity as the operative variable rather than formal policy typology (Round 13). The gradient is geographically uniform across district types - stronghold, swing, and proportional representation - ruling out electoral competition as a moderator (Round 6).

The Incorporation Gate

The content penalty operates at a precisely identified institutional chokepoint: the incorporation gate, where committees decide which member bills to consolidate into omnibus alternatives (Round 11). Small business bills clear this gate at roughly twice the rate of labor bills across assemblies (Round 11). For minimum wage legislation specifically, the omnibus pipeline never activates in three of six assemblies - 31 minimum wage bills were introduced in the 21st Assembly and all 31 expired without any committee decision (Rounds 3, 11). Once a bill enters the omnibus pipeline, it passes at 99.8 percent; the filtering is entirely upstream (Round 11).

Regime Contingency and the Two-Layer Architecture

The content gradient is not a structural constant. Under Roh Moo-hyun's progressive government, Labor Standards Act amendments processed at 89.7 percent; under Park Geun-hye's conservative presidency, the rate collapsed to 5.6 percent (Round 8). The forum's final theoretical architecture separates a conflict-avoidance baseline (all deliberative bodies under-process contentious items, accounting for approximately 34 percentage points of the gradient) from a partisan moderation layer (regime type shifts the contentious end by approximately 17 percentage points; Round 17). The partisan effect is bidirectional: conservative regimes suppress labor processing (down approximately 14 points) while simultaneously facilitating business regulation (up approximately 19 points for finance and firm regulation; Round 16). Small business processing remains regime-invariant at 43-62 percent while labor swings from 12 to 52 percent (Round 16).

The Environment and Labor Committee natural experiment confirmed this architecture: in the 17th Assembly, the committee treated labor and environment bills identically at 85 percent; from the 18th Assembly onward, a 30 percentage-point gap emerged and persisted (Round 19). The content penalty is a discrete level effect activated by institutional capacity constraints, not a continuous volume interaction (Round 19).

The Institutional Access Gate

The sponsor-committee match - whether a bill's sponsor serves on the committee to which the bill is referred - is the single most powerful predictor of bill processing, with a 15 percentage-point advantage that dwarfs all content variables (Round 6). Committee insiders face no average content penalty; outsiders face a significant 6.9 percentage-point disadvantage (Round 7). Yet neither insiders nor outsiders can escape the within-category Lowi gradient between labor and small business bills, confirming that institutional access and content operate as independent gates in a two-stage pipeline (Round 7).

Panel 2: Theoretical Contributions

The forum's 20 rounds constituted an extended theory tournament - a sequence of theoretical predictions proposed, tested, and either confirmed or rejected against the same dataset. The resulting theoretical architecture engages four distinct literatures and advances each.

Lowi (1964) and the distributive-redistributive distinction. The forum began by testing Lowi's prediction that redistributive policies face greater legislative friction than distributive ones. The prediction holds in the aggregate: redistributive and regulatory bills process at approximately 26 percent while distributive bills process at approximately 43 percent (Round 12). But the fine-grained test revealed that the gradient is continuous across seven sub-categories (Round 13), and the binary Lowi distinction misses the veterans anomaly - formally distributive legislation that processes like redistributive bills because it generates concentrated political opposition. The forum's contribution is to demonstrate that Lowi's typology identifies a real empirical pattern but misspecifies the mechanism: political conflict intensity, not formal policy type, predicts committee engagement. Wilson's (1980) cost-concentration framework performs better at the aggregate level but fails within-committee tests (Round 13), suggesting the operative variable is more granular than either typology captures.

Cox and McCubbins (2005) and partisan gatekeeping. The standard negative agenda power model predicts that majority-party leaders block bills that would divide their caucus. The forum found no partisan bias in committee processing under unified government, but a 10.9 percentage-point ruling-party advantage under divided government (Round 2). This finding initially appeared to support Cox-McCubbins, but the divided-government interaction collapsed when the sponsor-committee match was controlled (Round 6), and the content gradient persists under progressive committee chairs in the 22nd Assembly (Round 9). The forum's contribution is to bound the scope of partisan gatekeeping in the KNA: it is real but modest, content-selective rather than uniform, and secondary to institutional access and content friction (Round 16).

Hacker (2004), Patashnik (2008), and policy drift. The passive death finding - that 89-98 percent of non-productive bills expire through session termination without active rejection - places the KNA squarely in Hacker's policy drift framework, but with a crucial distinction. The drift documented here is neither by inaction in Hacker's sense (where existing policies erode as conditions change) nor by active erosion in Patashnik's sense (where enacted reforms are rolled back). It operates through what the forum termed "selective non-activation of an available institutional mechanism" (Round 12): committees possess the omnibus incorporation tool and deploy it efficiently for tractable domains while declining to activate it for contentious ones. This mechanism - drift by selective institutional deployment - is new to the literature.

Bachrach and Baratz (1962) and nondecision-making. The forum's deepest theoretical engagement is with the nondecision-making tradition. The 97.9 percent figure - passively dead bills with no committee processing record whatsoever - is a bill-level operationalization of what Bachrach and Baratz described as the "second face of power": the capacity to prevent issues from reaching the decision stage. Mapping the forum's findings onto Vij et al.'s (2024) nondecision-making typology, 61.7 percent of all member bills fall into "non-significant deliberation" (Round 19). The forum's contribution is to demonstrate that nondecision-making operates not only in weak institutional settings, as the classic literature assumed, but in formally obligated deliberative bodies with transparent procedures and public bill-tracking systems (Round 17). The three-dimensional measurement - probability of engagement, depth of deliberation, and duration of silence - provides the first quantitative operationalization of nondecision-making at the bill level in any legislature.

Discussant Response

The forum's methodological trajectory is itself a finding. Across 20 rounds, the project underwent 16 self-corrections - not evidence of failure, but evidence that adversarial multi-agent review works (Round 18). Each correction - from the sample-inflated Round 4 estimate to the withdrawn "structural invariance" claim to the reversed incorporation mechanism narrative - made the project more honest and more interesting (Round 9). The final evidence base is stronger for having been stress-tested by an agent whose explicit role was to find what was wrong.

What was done well. Four methodological strengths deserve recognition. First, the within-sponsor design (Round 4) is the forum's cleanest identification strategy: comparing bills introduced by the same legislator to different committees eliminates all legislator-level confounds and provides the most credible estimate of the content penalty. Second, the committee-routing validation (Rounds 8-9) - restricting analysis to bills whose policy content is confirmed by the committee to which they were routed - is methodologically innovative and produced the textbook signature of attenuation bias: the gradient amplified by 25-30 percent when measurement error was reduced. Third, the proportional representative test (Round 6) eliminates both district-level electoral incentives and position-taking simultaneously, providing the forum's most discriminating single test. Fourth, the supply-side null (Round 9) - demonstrating that labor and small business bills show no quality differences in text length, cosponsor counts, or other observable attributes - strengthens the demand-side interpretation by closing the most natural alternative explanation.

Remaining identification challenges. The project's central limitation is that it lacks an exogenous source of variation in bill content. The within-sponsor design controls for legislator-level confounds, and the committee fixed effects absorb time-invariant committee characteristics, but the assignment of policy content to bills is endogenous. If legislators strategically adjust the ambition or specificity of labor bills relative to small business bills - writing labor bills they know will fail, for instance - the observed gradient reflects strategic behavior rather than committee filtering. The five demand-side tests collectively make this interpretation less plausible, but none constitutes a definitive exclusion.

The regime-contingent framework rests on five assembly-level observations (Rounds 8, 19). The permutation test yields p = 0.10 - honest reporting that the forum deserves credit for, but insufficient for confident causal claims about regime effects. The confound between the 17th-to-18th Assembly transition and the simultaneous shift in bill volume and regime type remains unresolved (Round 19). A credible test would require either more assemblies (which time will provide) or a within-assembly source of regime variation (which the mid-term government transition in the 20th Assembly might supply).

The keyword classifier achieved only 44.2 percent coverage of the bill universe (Round 3). The committee-routing validation confirmed that classifier noise attenuates rather than inflates the gradient, which bounds the direction of bias but does not eliminate concern about heterogeneous misclassification across policy domains. The planned 350-bill hand-coded validation sample (Rounds 4, 8) was never completed. This remains the most actionable gap: a validated classifier would transform every estimate in both papers.

Finally, the committee chair partisan gatekeeping hypothesis - raised in every round from 2 through 19 - was never tested because official committee rosters were not obtained. This is the project's most conspicuous untested alternative explanation. If committee chairs from the ruling party systematically fast-track their party's bills in ways that correlate with content, the observed content gradient could partially reflect partisan selection rather than content friction.

Roundtable: Research Agenda

The forum's 20 rounds answered several questions definitively but opened at least as many. The following five research questions represent the highest-priority agenda for the next phase.

1. Does the content gradient survive a validated policy classifier? The committee-routing validation confirms the gradient is not an artifact, but a hand-coded sample of 350-500 bills, stratified by policy domain and committee, would provide the gold standard. This is the single highest-return investment for both papers: a validated classifier transforms every reported estimate from "lower bound" to "point estimate" and opens the door to machine-learning extensions that could classify the full universe of propose-reason texts.

2. Does committee chair partisanship mediate or moderate the content gradient? Official committee rosters crossed with chair party affiliation would enable a direct test of whether the content gradient operates through partisan gatekeeping (chairs block out-party content) or through a content-neutral institutional channel. The 22nd Assembly, where progressive chairs preside over historically low labor processing rates (2.3 percent), suggests the answer is not straightforward - but the test has never been run with proper data.

3. What happens inside the omnibus alternative? The forum identified the incorporation gate as the content-specific chokepoint but could not examine what happens after bills pass through it. Do omnibus alternatives that incorporate labor provisions retain their substantive ambition, or does incorporation serve as a face-saving mechanism that dilutes redistributive content? Full-text comparison of member bills and their corresponding committee alternatives would answer this question and potentially reveal a second stage of content filtering invisible to the current analysis.

4. Can the regime-contingent framework be tested with within-assembly variation? The 20th Assembly experienced a transition from unified progressive government (2016-2017) to divided government (2017-2020) following the presidential impeachment. This mid-term transition provides within-assembly variation in regime type while holding committee composition approximately constant - a natural experiment that could sharpen inference beyond the current five-assembly comparison.

5. Does nondecision-making operate comparably in other legislatures? The three-dimensional measurement framework (probability, depth, duration) is fully exportable. Applying it to the U.S. Congress, the Japanese Diet, or the European Parliament would test whether the KNA's nondecision-making pattern is a feature of presidential systems, of high-volume legislatures, or of deliberative institutions generally. The theoretical claim - that formally obligated deliberative bodies practice systematic nondecision-making along content lines - demands comparative evidence.

Conference Summary

Over 20 adversarial rounds, the KNA Research Agents forum constructed a bill-level account of how the Korean National Assembly processes - and fails to process - legislation. The central finding is that committee inaction is not random: bills addressing labor, welfare, and care populations face a systematic processing penalty that survives within-sponsor comparisons, committee fixed effects, and five independent tests against position-taking explanations. The penalty operates at the incorporation gate, where committees decide which member bills to consolidate into omnibus alternatives, and it varies dramatically across presidential regimes, from near-parity under progressive governments to near-total exclusion under conservative ones.

The theoretical contribution is a two-layer architecture of legislative nondecision-making. A conflict-avoidance baseline produces a continuous gradient in which politically tractable domains (small business, agriculture) receive routine committee engagement while contentious domains (labor, welfare) are met with institutional silence. A partisan moderation layer shifts the contentious end of this gradient according to regime type, suppressing labor processing under conservative governments while simultaneously facilitating business regulation. The mechanism is not active obstruction but selective non-activation: committees possess the institutional tools to process contentious legislation and deploy them efficiently for tractable domains while declining to activate them for others. Across the full dataset, 61.7 percent of all member bills receive no institutional trace of engagement, enduring a median 2.1 years of silence before expiring with the assembly term.

The project underwent 16 self-corrections across 20 rounds, each triggered by adversarial review. The divided-government headline from Round 4 was downsized when the sponsor-committee match was introduced in Round 6. The "structural invariance" claim from Round 7 was overturned by the regime-contingent evidence in Round 8. The "incorporation without output" narrative from Round 11 was corrected within the same round when omnibus alternatives were found to pass at 99.8 percent. What survived this gauntlet - the content gradient, the incorporation gate, the two-layer architecture - is more credible for having been attacked from within.
