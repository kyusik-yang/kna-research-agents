## Moderator Report

## Yeouido Agora Discussion Summary: Factions Within Parties

### 1. Stimulus
Intensifying factional conflicts within both the PPP (pro-Yoon vs. anti-Yoon) and the Democratic Party (pro-Lee vs. anti-Lee) raise the question of whether voters choose a party or a faction.

### 2. Sentiment Overview
Citizens across the political spectrum expressed frustration and disillusionment, converging on a shared perception that factional infighting crowds out substantive policymaking. Notably, even loyal partisan voters (Kwon Taesung, Park Sunhee) directed anger at their own party's internal divisions rather than at the opposing party. Cynicism about the meaningfulness of electoral choice was pervasive.

### 3. Key Themes
- **The transparency deficit**: Multiple participants argued the problem is not factionalism itself but its concealment. Yang Heejin's comparison to Japan's LDP - where factions are formalized - was echoed by Ryu Junhyeok's call for legally mandated disclosure of intra-party democracy metrics.
- **Structural reproduction of factions**: Im Hajin and Ryu Junhyeok identified nomination power concentration as the mechanism that perpetuates factional politics. "As long as nomination rights are concentrated in the party leader, factions are resource-allocation channels" (Im).
- **Livelihood disconnect**: Rural and working-class voices (Moon Changho, Park Sunhee, Hwang Jungae) framed factional conflict as zero-sum competition that diverts attention from local infrastructure, small-business survival, and agricultural subsidies.
- **Information asymmetry between voters and parties**: Baek Jongsu's principal-agent framing and Kang Soyeon's legal analysis both highlighted that voters formally choose parties but substantively empower unknown factional coalitions.

### 4. Demographic Patterns
Age mattered less than expected; young (Han Dongwook, 29) and old (Kwon Taesung, 71) conservatives shared cynicism, differing only in tone. The clearest divide was urban-analytical participants (Baek, Ryu, Yang) diagnosing institutional causes versus regional-livelihood participants (Moon, Park, Hwang) demanding tangible policy outcomes.

### 5. Communication Gaps
No participant engaged with how open primaries or decentralized nomination systems have performed empirically. The discussion assumed nomination-power concentration causes factional dominance but lacked awareness of comparative reform outcomes (e.g., U.S. primary systems generating their own pathologies).

### 6. Top Research Demands
1. Does the degree of nomination-power concentration measurably reduce legislative voting independence among proportional-representation members? (Im Hajin, Baek Jongsu)
2. Do policy outputs - bill passage rates, budget allocations - differ significantly when different factions control party leadership? (Han Dongwook)
3. Can proportional-representation candidate ranking be traced to factional bargaining rather than party-platform alignment? (Song Jaehyun, Park Sunhee)

### 7. Suggested Research Topic
`--topic "Nomination power concentration and legislative independence: comparing roll-call dissent rates under strong vs. weak party-leader authority in the 21st and 22nd Korean National Assembly"`
