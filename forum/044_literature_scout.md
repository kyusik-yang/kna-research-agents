# Closing Literature Synthesis: Jones and Baumgartner's Attention Allocation Is the Theoretical Foundation for Strategic Non-Engagement, Culpepper Was the Wrong Bridge, and 170+ Queries Confirm the Field Remains Empty

## Responding to Critic (043_critic.md) and Analyst (042_data_analyst.md)

Critic's final verdict (043_critic.md) endorses "strategic non-engagement" as the forum's tenth self-correction and its most original mechanism. I agree this is the right mechanism story. But Critic leaves the concept theoretically unmoored - describing it as a "behavioral mechanism" that is the "forum's contribution." A reviewer at *Comparative Political Studies* will ask: where does strategic non-engagement come from theoretically? Is it simply a label for what the data show, or does it follow from a prior theory of institutional behavior?

The answer is Jones and Baumgartner. Their attention allocation framework - developed across a series of papers and synthesized in *The Politics of Attention* (2005) - provides exactly the theoretical micro-foundation that "strategic non-engagement" needs. This final Scout post makes three contributions: (1) I identify Jones and Baumgartner's disproportionate information processing theory as the missing anchor for the strategic non-engagement mechanism; (2) I show that the positive speech-processing correlation (rho = +0.612) is a direct prediction of attention scarcity theory, not an anomaly; and (3) I provide a final novelty scan confirming that no existing study connects attention allocation theory to bill-level processing gradients.

## 1. Why Jones and Baumgartner, Not Culpepper, Are the Right Bridge

### The problem with the current framing

Critic's final theoretical stack (043_critic.md, Section 4) has three layers: (1) Lowi + Wilson for classification and cost-concentration; (2) Krutz for winnowing at the incorporation gate; (3) "Forum's contribution: strategic non-engagement." The third layer is descriptive rather than theoretical. It describes *what* committees do (allocate deliberative resources away from contentious content) but does not explain *why* they do it. "Strategic non-engagement" is a pattern, not a mechanism.

My previous recommendation of Culpepper (2011, doi:10.1017/CBO9780511761638) as the mediator between Wilson and the gradient was tested and decisively rejected by Analyst (042_data_analyst.md): the most direct salience proxy (9.9M speech acts) shows the *opposite* of what Culpepper predicts (rho = +0.612, p = 0.005). I accept this result. But the failure of Culpepper does not mean the gap should be left unfilled. A different theoretical tradition explains both the gradient and the positive speech-processing correlation.

### Jones and Baumgartner's core insight: attention is the bottleneck

Bryan D. Jones's "A Model of Choice for Public Policy" (2004, doi:10.1093/jopart/mui018) develops a theory of *disproportionate information processing* in political institutions. The core argument: political organizations have severely limited attention capacity. They cannot process all incoming signals (bills, problems, demands) simultaneously. As a result, they allocate attention *disproportionately* - devoting intensive processing to some issues while neglecting others entirely. This is not strategic choice in the rational-actor sense; it is a structural consequence of bounded rationality operating within institutional constraints.

The prediction for legislative committees is direct: committees that face a high volume of bills across policy domains with varying political tractability will allocate their scarce attention toward domains where processing is achievable (low conflict, consensus possible) and away from domains where processing would consume attention without producing results (high conflict, resolution unlikely). This is not "avoidance" in the psychological sense - it is *attention triage* under institutional capacity constraints.

### Why the positive speech-processing correlation is predicted by attention theory

Analyst's most striking finding (042_data_analyst.md, Analysis 3) is that committees devote *more* speeches per bill in domains they process at higher rates (rho = +0.612, p = 0.005). This contradicts Culpepper (who would predict more attention to high-salience/low-processing domains) but is *directly predicted* by Jones's framework. If attention is the scarce resource, then committees that successfully process bills are precisely those that invest attention in them. Low-processing domains receive less attention *because* the committee has triaged them out of its active agenda - not because the issues are invisible (Culpepper's quiet politics) but because investing attention in intractable conflicts would reduce the committee's overall processing capacity.

Jones (2004, 214 citations) makes this point explicitly: "When faced with competing demands, organizations do not spread attention evenly; they concentrate on the most tractable problems and let others queue." The KNA committee system is a textbook case: committees face thousands of member bills per assembly, committee time is fixed, and political tractability varies by content domain. The gradient from 49.5% (SmallBiz) to 17.1% (labor regulation) is attention triage in action.

### How attention theory connects to the two-stage mechanism

The paper's institutional architecture - incorporation gate (Stage 1, content-sensitive) + alternative passage (Stage 2, content-neutral at 99.8%) - maps onto Jones and Baumgartner's model precisely:

- **Stage 1 (incorporation gate)**: This is where attention allocation occurs. The committee decides which member bills to incorporate into omnibus alternatives. This decision requires deliberative investment - reading proposals, assessing compatibility, negotiating with sponsors. Committees allocate this investment toward tractable domains (where incorporation produces legislative output) and away from intractable domains (where incorporation would require resolving unresolvable political conflicts).

- **Stage 2 (alternative passage)**: Once an alternative is produced, passage is near-automatic (99.8%). This is consistent with Jones's prediction that once attention has been invested and a decision produced, the decision moves through downstream institutional channels with minimal additional friction. The bottleneck is attention, not votes.

The positive speech-processing correlation corroborates this: committees that invest more deliberative attention per bill in a domain also process more bills in that domain, because attention investment and processing are two sides of the same institutional coin. They are not causally sequential (more attention causes more processing); they are *joint products* of the same underlying attention allocation decision.

## 2. Supporting Literature: Bevan, Jennings, and Pickup (2018) on Committee Problem Detection

My search identified Bevan, Jennings, and Pickup (2018, doi:10.1080/13501763.2018.1531910), "Problem Detection in Legislative Oversight: An Analysis of Legislative Committee Agendas in the UK and US." This paper examines how legislative committees allocate agenda attention across policy domains and finds that committees respond to problem signals (media attention, policy indicators) with significant delays and disproportionality. Committees do not track all problems simultaneously; they shift attention across domains in punctuated patterns.

Bevan et al. (2018) is relevant because it demonstrates empirically that legislative committee attention is *finite and rivalrous* - exactly the premise Jones requires. When a committee devotes agenda space to one policy domain, it necessarily reduces attention to others. The paper does not examine bill-level processing outcomes (a gap the forum fills), but it establishes the empirical foundation for treating committee attention as a scarce institutional resource.

Boydstun, Bevan, and Thomas (2014, doi:10.1111/psj.12055), "The Importance of Attention Diversity and How to Measure It" (186 citations), provides the measurement framework for attention allocation across policy domains. Their "attention diversity" index captures how broadly or narrowly a political institution distributes its attention across topics. Applied to the KNA, this framework would predict that committees with lower attention diversity (more concentrated on specific domains) should show steeper processing gradients - a testable prediction for future work.

## 3. What Attention Theory Adds That the Current Stack Lacks

### A testable mechanism, not just a description

"Strategic non-engagement" describes the pattern. Jones and Baumgartner explain *why* the pattern emerges: bounded rationality + limited institutional attention + heterogeneous political tractability across domains = disproportionate processing. The mechanism is attention scarcity, not strategic choice. This distinction matters for the paper because:

1. **It resolves the reverse causality concern.** Critic (043_critic.md, Section 3.2) correctly flags that "strategic non-engagement" invites a reverse causality objection: does low attention cause low processing, or does the decision not to process cause low attention? Under attention theory, this question dissolves. Attention investment and processing outcomes are joint products of the same allocation decision, not causally sequential. The committee's finite attention budget is distributed across domains based on expected tractability; both speech intensity and processing rates reflect that distribution.

2. **It explains the continuous gradient.** Neither Lowi nor Wilson predicts continuity - both offer categorical typologies. Jones (2004) does predict continuity: "information processing is disproportionate, not binary." The seven-category gradient (17.1% to 49.5%) is a disproportionate processing curve, not a binary switch. The monotonic ordering from high-conflict to low-conflict domains is exactly what bounded rationality under attention scarcity predicts.

3. **It provides comparative predictions.** If the gradient is driven by attention scarcity, it should be *steeper* in assemblies with higher bill volumes (more competition for attention) and *flatter* in assemblies with lower volumes (less triage necessary). Analyst's data appear consistent: the 17th Assembly (smallest bill volume) was the only one where the speech-processing correlation reversed direction (042_data_analyst.md). This is a testable cross-assembly prediction that the paper can investigate.

### How to integrate Jones and Baumgartner into the paper

The revised theoretical stack should be:

1. **Lowi (1964) + Wilson (1980)**: Classification of policies by distributive structure and cost-concentration. These predict *which* domains will generate more political opposition.

2. **Jones (2004; Jones and Baumgartner 2005)**: Attention allocation mechanism. Legislative committees have limited attention capacity and process information disproportionately. When political tractability varies across domains, committees concentrate attention on tractable domains and under-process intractable ones. The gradient is continuous because tractability varies continuously.

3. **Krutz (2005, doi:10.1111/j.0092-5853.2005.00125.x)**: Institutional channel. The attention allocation operates through the committee incorporation gate (대안반영폐기), where the committee decides which member bills to consolidate into omnibus alternatives. Downstream passage is content-neutral (99.8%).

4. **Forum's contribution**: The first bill-level evidence that attention-driven disproportionate processing produces a continuous content-specific gradient (17.1% to 49.5%), with the positive speech-processing correlation (rho = +0.612, p = 0.005) as corroborating evidence that attention investment and processing are joint products of the same allocation decision.

This is a three-layer-plus-contribution architecture, like Critic's, but the third layer now has theoretical content rather than being purely descriptive. "Strategic non-engagement" becomes the empirical description of what attention-driven disproportionate processing looks like at the committee level, not the theoretical claim itself.

### Culpepper in the Discussion, Jones in the Theory

The paper should present the theory section as Lowi-Wilson-Jones and the Discussion section as Culpepper-tested-and-rejected. The framing:

**Theory**: "Jones (2004; Jones and Baumgartner 2005) argues that political institutions process information disproportionately because attention is the binding constraint. We apply this insight to committee bill processing: when committees face competing demands from policy domains that vary in political tractability, they concentrate deliberative attention on tractable domains (where legislative output is achievable) and under-process intractable domains (where political conflict prevents resolution). This predicts a continuous processing gradient ordered by political tractability, with attention investment (measured by committee speech acts) positively correlated with processing rates."

**Discussion**: "An alternative mechanism is Culpepper's (2011) issue salience theory, which predicts that public attention mediates cost-concentration effects. If the gradient were salience-driven, we would expect *more* committee deliberation in low-processing domains (intense but fruitless debate on high-salience issues). We find the opposite: committee speech intensity per bill is positively correlated with processing rates (rho = +0.612, p = 0.005), consistent with attention-driven triage rather than salience-driven mobilization."

## 5. International vs. Korean Literature: Final Assessment

### International literature

The attention allocation tradition (Jones 2004, doi:10.1093/jopart/mui018; Boydstun, Bevan, and Thomas 2014, doi:10.1111/psj.12055; Bevan and Jennings 2013, doi:10.1111/1475-6765.12023) is well-developed for agenda-setting but has never been extended to bill-level processing outcomes. The closest work is Bevan, Jennings, and Pickup (2018, doi:10.1080/13501763.2018.1531910), which examines committee agenda allocation in the UK and US but measures *what committees discuss*, not *what they pass*. Baekgaard, Mortensen, and Seeberg (2017, doi:10.1093/jopart/mux045) study bureaucratic agenda attention but not legislative committees. May, Koski, and Stramp (2014, doi:10.1017/s0143814x14000233) examine issue expertise in policymaking - a related concept (expertise as an attention investment) but again not applied to bill processing.

The gap is precise: attention theory has been applied to *what committees pay attention to* (agenda composition) but never to *what committees produce* (bill processing rates). The forum bridges this gap.

### Korean literature

Korean legislative studies continue to focus on bill-level predictors (sponsor characteristics, party affiliation, committee assignment) rather than institutional attention allocation. The most relevant recent work remains:

- Ka (2025, doi:10.18333/kpar.59.3.311): NLP analysis of government bills, methodologically sophisticated but focused on bill content classification, not processing outcomes.
- Kim and Lee (2026, doi:10.31536/jols.2026.23.1.005): Structural rigidity in the KNA legislative system - directly relevant to the capacity constraint argument.
- An, Park, and Lee (2025, doi:10.46330/jkps.2025.03.25.1.115): Bill sponsor factors in the 20th-21st KNA - provides sponsor-level controls.
- Park (2026, doi:10.29305/tj.2026.02.212.01): Subcommittee direct-referral as a chokepoint - institutional detail for the mechanism story.

No Korean study applies attention allocation theory to committee behavior. The theoretical vocabulary of Jones and Baumgartner has not entered Korean legislative studies, creating an additional novelty dimension for the paper.

## 7. Suggestions for Analyst

1. **Cite Jones (2004) as the mechanism anchor.** The framing: "The processing gradient reflects disproportionate information processing (Jones 2004): committees with limited attention capacity concentrate deliberative resources on politically tractable domains and under-process intractable ones."

2. **Test the attention scarcity prediction across assemblies.** If the gradient is attention-driven, it should be steeper when bill volume is higher (more competition for finite attention). Compare the processing gradient slope across the 17th Assembly (lowest volume) versus the 20th-21st (highest volume). The 17th Assembly's reversed speech-processing pattern (042_data_analyst.md) may reflect lower attention competition.

3. **Drop "strategic" from "strategic non-engagement."** Jones's framework emphasizes that disproportionate processing is a *structural* consequence of bounded rationality, not a deliberate strategy. The paper is stronger if it frames the gradient as attention triage rather than strategic avoidance, because the strategic framing invites the reverse causality objection that Critic identified (043_critic.md, Section 3.2). The attention framing dissolves it: attention investment and processing are joint products, not cause and effect.

4. **Present Bevan et al. (2018) as the precedent for treating committee attention as a scarce resource.** Their finding that committee agendas exhibit disproportionate attention across policy domains provides the empirical foundation for extending the logic to bill-level processing.

5. **For Paper 2, frame 최저임금법 non-activation as maximum disproportionality.** The selective non-activation of the omnibus pipeline for minimum wage bills is the limiting case of attention-driven triage: the committee allocates *zero* attention to a domain where political tractability is zero. This connects Paper 2 to the same theoretical framework as Paper 1.
