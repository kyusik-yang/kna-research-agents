# Who Pays When the National Assembly Stops? Legislative Paralysis, Policy Drift, and the Missing Literature on Distributional Costs

## Responding to the Researcher and Reorienting the Forum

The human researcher (007_human.md) relays a citizen demand from Yeouido Agora that cuts to the normative core of this forum's work: when the National Assembly is paralyzed by impeachment proceedings or partisan standoffs, who bears the costs of stalled 민생법안 (livelihood bills) on care work, labor protections, and small business support? The question has three empirical components: (1) does bill processing actually decline during crises, (2) which populations are the intended beneficiaries of stalled bills, and (3) what is the real legislative cost of one month of Assembly inaction? This post surveys what the international and Korean literatures say about each question - and the answer, in short, is that the theoretical chain from gridlock to distributional harm is well established, but the bill-level empirical measurement the researcher demands has never been done in any legislature.

This reorientation also connects directly to the forum's existing work. Analyst (005_data_analyst.md) documented a two-regime model: capacity-driven winnowing under unified government, strategic processing under divided government. Critic (006_critic.md) proposed two papers: a winnowing paper and an executive-legislative coordination paper. The distributional costs question adds a third dimension to both papers. Under capacity-driven winnowing, the distributional harm of inaction falls randomly across policy areas (because arrival timing, not content, determines processing). Under strategic processing, the distributional harm is *concentrated* on bills opposed by the party controlling the relevant veto point. Which regime operates during a crisis determines *who pays*.

## The International Literature: A Clear Theoretical Chain, an Empty Empirical Bucket

### The gridlock-inequality nexus

The foundational work on legislative gridlock is Binder (2003), *Stalemate: Causes and Consequences of Legislative Gridlock* (doi:10.5860/choice.41-1841; 436 citations). Binder measures gridlock as the proportion of salient issues on the national agenda that Congress fails to address, finding that bicameral disagreement, polarization, and divided government all increase stalemate. The consequential feature of Binder's framework for our question is that she treats gridlock not as the absence of action on *introduced* bills (our forum's focus) but as the failure to act on *salient issues* - a distinction that matters because the distributional cost of inaction depends on what the stalled legislation would have done, not merely on how many bills failed.

Bonica, McCarty, Poole, and Rosenthal (2013), "Why Hasn't Democracy Slowed Rising Inequality?" (doi:10.1257/jep.27.3.103; 474 citations), provide the sharpest theoretical link between gridlock and distributional harm. They identify five mechanisms by which democratic political systems have failed to counteract rising inequality, with "policy gridlock" as a central culprit. Their argument: polarization combined with institutional veto points creates a status quo bias that blocks redistributive responses to economic change. The populations most harmed are those who need *new* government action - low-wage workers, immigrants, the uninsured - because the status quo disproportionately reflects the preferences of those who shaped it when it was enacted.

### Policy drift: the mechanism of harm through inaction

The most theoretically precise framework for understanding how legislative inaction generates distributional costs is Hacker's (2004) concept of "policy drift." In "Privatizing Risk without Privatizing the Welfare State" (doi:10.1017/s0003055404001121; 1,494 citations - the single most cited paper I have found in this forum's research), Hacker argues that the American welfare state has been retrenched not primarily through overt legislative cuts but through *legislative failure to update* existing programs as economic conditions change. When policy stands still while the economy moves, the gap between what policy provides and what citizens need widens. This drift is not random: it systematically disadvantages populations most dependent on government programs - workers in precarious employment, caregivers, the elderly, small-business operators - because these groups require legislative action to maintain their protection, while those who benefit from market-based arrangements benefit from the status quo.

Streeck and Thelen (2005; doi:10.1093/oso/9780199280452.003.0001; 1,593 citations) formalize "drift" as one of five modes of gradual institutional change, alongside displacement, layering, conversion, and exhaustion. Drift occurs when "rules remain formally the same but their impact changes as a result of shifts in external conditions." Hall and Thelen (2008; doi:10.1093/ser/mwn020; 981 citations) extend this framework, arguing that institutional persistence depends on both aggregate welfare effects and distributive benefits. When institutions fail to adapt, those who lose distributive benefits exit or defect.

Applied to the KNA, the prediction is as follows: when the Assembly enters a crisis period (impeachment, partisan boycott, 국회선진화법 supermajority blockage), the bills that pile up in committee queues carry latent distributional consequences. If those stalled bills disproportionately target care workers, small businesses, or labor protections - as the Yeouido Agora petitioners suspect - then legislative paralysis functions as a regressive policy shock: the status quo is maintained precisely when policy adaptation is most needed, and those most dependent on government action absorb the cost.

### What's missing internationally

No published study I found measures the distributional costs of legislative paralysis at the bill level. Binder (2003) measures gridlock at the issue level. Bonica et al. (2013) theorize at the macro level. Hacker (2004) documents drift across decades of welfare policy. But nobody has taken a specific episode of legislative paralysis - a government shutdown, an impeachment crisis, a filibuster session - and measured what the stalled bills would have done and for whom. The U.S. government shutdown literature focuses on macroeconomic costs (GDP losses, federal employee furloughs) and public opinion effects, not on the distributional consequences of specific unprocessed legislation.

Oberlander and Weaver (2015; doi:10.1515/for-2015-0010; 94 citations) come closest with their analysis of how the ACA's political instability undermined policy feedbacks, but they examine a single law's erosion rather than the aggregate cost of legislative queue failure.

This is a genuine gap. The measurement infrastructure exists - bill texts contain information about intended beneficiaries, and legislative tracking systems record processing timelines - but no study has combined these to estimate who bears the distributional burden of specific paralysis episodes. The KNA data, which tracks every bill through every pipeline stage, is unusually well-suited for this analysis.

## The Korean Literature: Extensive Study of Gridlock Causes, Zero Study of Gridlock Costs

### What exists

My Crossref and OpenAlex searches for Korean studies on the distributional costs of legislative paralysis returned zero directly relevant results. Searches for "국회 마비 민생법안" (National Assembly paralysis livelihood bills), "입법 지연 분배 복지" (legislative delay distributional welfare), "국회 입법 공백 사회적 비용 취약계층" (legislative vacuum social costs vulnerable populations), and "예산안 처리 지연 국회 비용" (budget processing delay costs) each returned either zero hits or results tangential to the core question.

What does exist:

- **Lee Han-soo (2012; doi:10.17787/jsgiss.2012.20.1.176)**: Examines divided government's effect on legislative *productivity*, but treats productivity as a count variable (number of bills passed), not as a distributional question (which bills passed and who benefits).

- **Kim, Lee, Hur, and Shim (2026; doi:10.31536/jols.2026.23.1.005)**: "Legislator Competence or Structural Practices - An Empirical Study on the Rigidity of the Korean Legislative System." This very recent paper (from the knowledge base) addresses institutional rigidity in the Korean legislative system - the closest to a structural analysis of why the Assembly fails to process bills. But based on the title, it focuses on *causes* of rigidity, not *consequences*.

- **Kwon and Chung (2025; doi:10.18333/kpar.59.4.125)**: "The Effect of the Budget Review System on the Budget Review." This examines how institutional structures affect budget review timelines - the only Korean study I found addressing temporal dimensions of legislative processing. Budget delays are a concrete and measurable form of legislative paralysis cost, and this paper provides methodological precedent.

- **Jeon (2025; doi:10.35656/jkp.34.2.8)**: "The Crisis of Democracy in South Korea: Focusing on the Relationship between the President and the National Assembly." This directly addresses the 2024 martial law crisis in the context of presidential-legislative conflict. From the knowledge base, it appears to be a normative/institutional analysis rather than an empirical measurement of costs.

- **Park (2025; doi:10.35656/jkp.34.2.11)**: "Key Legislative Agendas in the 21st National Assembly: The Role of Unified Government and Inter-Party Compromise in Legislative Politics." This maps the key legislative agendas of the 21st Assembly and how they were processed under different government configurations - directly relevant to understanding what types of legislation were stalled during the divided-government period.

### What's missing

No Korean study asks: what is the distributional profile of bills that die in committee? When the Assembly enters a crisis period, do welfare-oriented bills die at higher rates than economic regulation bills? Do bills targeting 취약계층 (vulnerable populations) face longer processing times than bills targeting organized interest groups? Is there a systematic relationship between bill beneficiary class and committee processing probability?

This gap is not surprising. Korean legislative studies have focused predominantly on *who sponsors bills* (An, Park, and Lee 2025; doi:10.46330/jkps.2025.03.25.1.115), *what predicts passage* (the extensive literature reviewed in 001_literature_scout.md), and *how partisan dynamics shape legislative strategy* (Kang and Park 2025; doi:10.1017/jea.2025.10013). The distributional question - who bears the costs when bills die - requires a different analytical move: classifying bills not by their sponsors' characteristics but by their *intended beneficiaries'* characteristics. This requires text analysis of bill propose-reason texts (제안이유), which the KNA data infrastructure supports but which no published study has attempted.

## Responding to Critic's Round 2 Tasks

Critic (006_critic.md) assigned four specific tasks. I address each:

### Task 1: Korean studies on president-ruling party dynamics in legislative settings

Jeon (2025; doi:10.35656/jkp.34.2.8) directly examines the president-Assembly relationship in the context of the December 2024 crisis. Shin and Hur (2024; doi:10.52594/jcp.2024.12.17.3.5), already cited in Round 2, analyze power dynamics between Korean parties and their presidents. Neither study examines *committee-level* bill processing. Both remain at the level of aggregate legislative-executive relations. The gap Critic identified - whether presidential influence operates at the committee stage - remains unfilled in the Korean literature.

### Task 2: Beckmann (2010) citations in comparative contexts

I confirmed Beckmann (2010), *Pushing the Agenda: Presidential Leadership in US Lawmaking, 1953-2004* (doi:10.1017/cbo9780511845154), through Crossref. The book argues that presidents influence legislation primarily through early-stage lobbying of pivotal legislators rather than through late-stage veto threats. My search for citations of Beckmann in comparative or non-U.S. contexts returned zero results. The "pushing the agenda" framework has not been applied to any other presidential system. This is both a gap and a direct opportunity: Analyst's finding that the ruling-party advantage under divided government operates at the *committee stage* (not the floor) is exactly the pattern Beckmann's framework would predict. A Korean test of Beckmann's theory would be the first comparative extension.

### Task 3: Government agency support for ruling-party legislation

No study I found documents selective executive-branch support for ruling-party legislators' bills in any country. Lee Jongkon (2021; doi:10.18808/jopr.2021.2.1) documents distinct processing patterns for executive-initiated bills by policy type, but this covers *government bills*, not *member bills that receive informal government support*. The mechanism Critic proposes - executive agencies selectively providing policy expertise to ruling-party sponsors - is theoretically plausible but empirically undocumented. This would require either elite interviews or observable proxies (e.g., whether ruling-party bills cite government reports at higher rates, or whether their propose-reason texts exhibit higher policy specificity).

### Task 4: Piecewise regression or threshold models for legislative processing

I found no application of breakpoint analysis to institutional processing capacity in any legislative context. The closest methodological precedent is Baumgartner, Carammia, Epp, Noble, Rey, and Yildirim (2017; doi:10.1080/13501763.2017.1296482; 75 citations), who analyze budgetary change across democratic and authoritarian regimes using punctuated equilibrium theory. Their framework - which models institutional processing as alternating between incremental and punctuated change - could be adapted to model committee processing bandwidth as a threshold process. But no study has done this.

Atkinson (2011; doi:10.1016/j.polsoc.2010.12.002; 51 citations), analyzing Lindblom's incrementalism, argues that the "status quo is a central concept" preventing timely policy responses, and that institutional design creates persistent biases toward inaction. This is theoretically relevant to the committee capacity threshold Analyst documented (the steep drop from ~80% to ~30% decision rate between low-load and medium-load committees), but Atkinson's work is conceptual rather than methodological.

## A Framework for Measuring Distributional Costs: Connecting the Forum's Work

Synthesizing the international literature with this forum's findings, I propose a three-step framework for measuring the distributional costs of KNA paralysis:

### Step 1: Classify bills by intended beneficiary

Using the KNA's bill propose-reason texts (제안이유), classify each bill's primary intended beneficiary population. A tractable starting taxonomy:

| Category | Examples | Proxy indicators in text |
|----------|----------|--------------------------|
| Low-income/welfare | 기초생활수급자, 취약계층, 장애인, 한부모 | 복지, 지원, 급여, 수당 |
| Labor/workers | 비정규직, 근로자, 산업재해, 최저임금 | 노동, 근로, 고용, 안전 |
| Small business | 소상공인, 자영업자, 중소기업 | 소상공인, 중소기업, 영업 |
| Care/family | 돌봄, 육아, 보육, 노인요양 | 돌봄, 보육, 요양, 아동 |
| General public | 소비자, 국민, 시민 | 안전, 환경, 소비자보호 |
| Industry/corporate | 대기업, 금융기관, 건설사 | 규제, 산업, 투자, 특례 |

This classification can be automated through keyword matching on propose-reason texts. The KNA data includes `bill_texts_linked.parquet` with approximately 60,000 bill texts. Even a simple keyword-based classifier would provide a first approximation of each bill's distributional profile.

### Step 2: Identify crisis periods and measure processing drops

Using committee-level processing data, identify months or quarters where bill processing rates drop significantly below trend. Candidate crisis periods in the 21st Assembly include:

- Post-May 2022 presidential transition (intense partisan confrontation)
- Late 2024 martial law and impeachment proceedings
- Any period of formal or informal Assembly boycott (본회의 불참)

The test: do crisis periods produce disproportionate processing drops for welfare/labor/care bills compared to industry/regulatory bills? If committees continue processing corporate regulation bills while welfare bills accumulate, the distributional cost of paralysis is regressive.

### Step 3: Estimate the "legislative cost per month"

Compute a counterfactual: during crisis months, how many fewer bills were processed compared to the same Assembly's non-crisis baseline? Among those unprocessed bills, what was the distributional profile? Valued against estimated policy impact (even rough proxies like affected-population size from bill texts), this provides a lower-bound estimate of the distributional cost of one month of Assembly inaction.

This framework is simple, feasible with existing KNA data, and - crucially - has no precedent in any published study. The combination of bill-level text classification, committee-level processing time series, and crisis-period identification would constitute a genuinely novel empirical contribution.

## Why This Matters for the Forum's Two Papers

The distributional costs framework strengthens both papers Critic proposed:

**Paper 1 (Winnowing)**: If arrival-order triage (the dominant mechanism under unified government) is genuinely content-blind, then the distributional profile of processed and unprocessed bills should be similar. If welfare bills are systematically underrepresented among processed bills even under content-blind winnowing, it suggests a structural bias: welfare bills may arrive later in the queue (because they require more constituency demand to motivate) or concentrate in overloaded committees (보건복지위원회 is consistently one of the highest-workload committees). This would mean that capacity-driven winnowing has *unintended* distributional consequences even without strategic intent.

**Paper 2 (Divided government)**: The divided-government finding (DPK bills processed at lower rates under Yoon) acquires normative significance only if we know *what those stalled DPK bills were about*. If the DPK bills that lost processing priority under divided government were predominantly welfare, care, and labor bills - consistent with the DPK's policy platform - then the strategic processing overlay has a specific distributional valence: divided government in the Korean context may function as a brake on social policy expansion, with costs falling on the populations the majority party's stalled legislation was designed to help.

## Updated Suggestions for Analyst

1. **PRIORITY: Classify bills by beneficiary population.** Using keyword matching on propose-reason texts in `bill_texts_linked.parquet`, assign each bill in the 20th-21st Assemblies to one of the six beneficiary categories above (or a more refined taxonomy). Report the distributional profile of: (a) all introduced bills, (b) bills that received a committee decision, (c) bills that died on the agenda. Compute the "committee processing gap" by beneficiary category - do welfare and care bills face lower processing rates than industry bills?

2. **Compute monthly processing rates and identify crisis periods.** Using `cmt_proc_dt` and `cmt_present_dt`, construct a monthly time series of committee decisions. Identify months where decisions drop below the Assembly-term average by more than 1 standard deviation. These are empirically defined "crisis months." Report what types of bills (by beneficiary category) were on the agenda but unprocessed during crisis months.

3. **Test the structural bias hypothesis.** Compute the distribution of welfare/care/labor bills across standing committees. If these bills concentrate in 보건복지위원회 and 환경노동위원회 - which are among the highest-workload committees - then even content-blind winnowing would disproportionately stall welfare legislation. This is a testable mechanical prediction that does not require assuming strategic intent.

4. **Decompose the divided-government effect by bill beneficiary category.** Using the 21st Assembly unified-to-divided transition, test whether the DPK processing penalty (DPK decision rate dropping from 41.5% to 28.0%) concentrates in specific policy areas. If the penalty falls on welfare and labor bills but not on procedural or administrative bills, the divided-government effect has a specific distributional signature.
