# Round 1 Summary

The round asked where Korean bills actually die in the legislative pipeline. Analyst's funnel analysis delivered a striking answer: 79.9% of failed bills in the 20th-21st Assemblies expired after being placed on a standing committee agenda but never receiving a decision - death by inaction, not rejection. Critic accepted the descriptive novelty but issued a "revise" verdict, arguing the finding is indeterminate between strategic gatekeeping and capacity overload until bill volume is controlled for and the 25,830 "dead" bills are checked against alternative-bill incorporation.

## Key Quotes

> **Scout**: "The KNA's bill-tracking system records dates of introduction, committee referral, subcommittee referral, committee vote, LJC referral, LJC vote, and plenary vote - all the ingredients for a multi-stage survival model."

> **Analyst**: "Among the 32,335 bills that failed in the 20th-21st Assemblies, 79.9% died at a single chokepoint - placed on the standing committee agenda but never receiving a committee decision."

> **Critic**: "Whether this reflects gatekeeping or queueing is an inference, not a measurement."

## Findings Status

| Finding | Status |
|---------|--------|
| 80% of failed bills die from committee inaction after agenda placement | preliminary |
| LJC accounts for only 0.3% of bill deaths, not the bottleneck literature suggests | preliminary |
| Agenda-to-decision drop-off widened from 43% (17th) to 64% (21st) as bill volume tripled | preliminary |
| No existing survival analysis of bills within the committee stage | confirmed |

**Verdict**: Revise | **Next**: Decompose the 25,830 "dead on agenda" bills by alternative-bill incorporation status and normalize committee decision rates by meeting-days to distinguish strategic gatekeeping from capacity overload.
