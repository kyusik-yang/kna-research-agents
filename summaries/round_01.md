# Round 1 Summary

## Focus

Round 1 established the empirical and theoretical foundations for studying committee gatekeeping in the Korean National Assembly (KNA). The central question: why do the vast majority of bills die in the KNA, and what role do standing committees play in this filtering process? The round produced a literature map, a descriptive data anatomy of 110K+ bills across the 17th - 22nd Assemblies, and a methodological critique proposing a path toward causal identification.

## Agent Contributions

**Scout (Literature Tracker)** mapped the international and Korean literatures on committee gatekeeping, identifying Cox and McCubbins's cartel theory and Krehbiel's informational theory as the competing frameworks. Scout documented five specific gaps, the most important being that no study systematically quantifies passage rate variation across KNA standing committees. The Korean literature focuses on bill-level and legislator-level attributes (sponsor identity, party affiliation) rather than committee-level filtering mechanisms.

**Analyst (KNA Data Expert)** filled the central gap with committee-level tabulations. Key findings: passage rates range from 11.0% to 39.2% across standing committees in the 21st Assembly; 63.4% of bills die by term expiration (not explicit rejection); and the Legislation and Judiciary Committee (법사위), despite its reputation as a second veto gate, has a near-100% pass-through rate in its review capacity. Analyst also documented the 대안반영 (alternative-bill absorption) pathway as the KNA's dominant mode of actual legislating, with 23% of 21st Assembly bills absorbed into committee alternatives.

**Critic (Theory & Methods)** scored the work as "revise" - strong descriptive foundation but lacking causal identification. Critic flagged three confounds in the committee comparisons (bill composition, policy domain, workload saturation) and argued that the "death by inaction" pattern is consistent with both cartel and informational theories. Critic proposed a two-paper research agenda: (1) a difference-in-differences design exploiting mid-term committee chair rotations to test partisan gatekeeping, and (2) a study of the 대안반영 pathway as a Korean form of omnibus legislating.

## Points of Agreement

All three agents agree that committee-level decomposition of bill survival is genuinely novel for Korea. They concur that term expiration - not rejection - is the dominant mode of bill death, and that proposer type (committee chair > government > member) is the strongest predictor of passage. The 대안반영 pathway was recognized by all as theoretically important and understudied.

## Points of Disagreement

Analyst interprets the data as broadly supporting the cartel (negative agenda power) framework; Critic counters that position-taking bills, capacity saturation, and strategic self-censorship produce identical observable patterns, making the cartel claim untestable without exogenous variation in committee control. Critic also questions the novelty of the 법사위 rubber-stamp finding, noting that Korean legal scholars have long debated this qualitatively.

## Most Promising Research Directions

1. **Committee chair partisan effects via DiD** - exploiting mid-term chair rotations as identifying variation
2. **대안반영 as committee-level omnibus legislating** - testing whether absorption systematically favors ruling-party bills
3. **Competing risks survival analysis** extended beyond the 18th Assembly, with 대안반영 as a distinct event type
4. **Fast-track reform (안건신속처리제) effect** - pre/post comparison of bill survival dynamics

## What Remains Unresolved

- Committee chair identity and party affiliation data are not in the bill database and must be collected externally
- The 법사위 dual-recording ambiguity (conflicting timestamp fields) needs cross-validation before firm conclusions
- No linkage exists between 대안반영 bills and their target alternatives
- The causal mechanism behind cross-committee passage rate variation is entirely unidentified
- Whether the observed patterns reflect partisan gatekeeping, informational screening, or capacity constraints cannot be distinguished with current evidence
