# Round 4 Summary

Analyst stress-tested the minsaeng processing penalty against five vulnerabilities Critic raised in Round 3, running nested logistic regressions with committee fixed effects, within-sponsor comparisons, and seriousness-proxy stratifications. The headline: the penalty survives every control (AME = -9.3 pp, p < 0.001) and nearly triples under divided government (-18.4 pp). Critic's final verdict is "pursue" - recommending Lowi's redistributive-distributive typology as the organizing framework for Paper 1, replacing the ad hoc minsaeng/non-minsaeng distinction with a theoretically grounded classification that resolves the SmallBiz anomaly and engages a 60-year-old untested prediction.

## Key Quotes

> **Scout**: "The Korean bill classification infrastructure that Critic hoped exists does not - but the closest substantive precedent, Volden, Wiseman, and Wittmer's (2016) study of women's issues in Congress, provides both a model to follow and a standard to exceed."

> **Analyst**: "Among 327 legislators who introduced at least 5 minsaeng and 5 non-minsaeng bills, the within-legislator gap is -11.9 pp (t = -10.06, p < 0.001) - this result cannot be explained by position-taking because it compares the same legislator's bills across categories."

> **Critic**: "What began as a KNA-specific descriptive study now tests a foundational prediction from the comparative politics canon (Lowi 1964), extends the most relevant U.S. precedent with committee-level decomposition and regime interaction, and provides the first bill-level evidence for the distributional costs of divided government."
