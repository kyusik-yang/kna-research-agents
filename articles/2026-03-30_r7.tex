\begin{abstract}
\noindent When legislatures cannot process every bill, what determines which survive the committee stage? I argue that committee processing operates through two independent gates: an institutional gate, where a sponsor's membership on the reviewing committee substantially raises processing probability regardless of bill content, and a content gate, where redistributive legislation faces structural friction regardless of the sponsor's institutional position. I test this two-level architecture using 12,889 content-classified bills from the Korean National Assembly (20th and 21st terms, 2016 to 2024). Committee membership emerges as the dominant predictor of processing, roughly five times more powerful than policy content. Yet the Lowi gradient, the gap between redistributive labor bills and distributive small-business bills, persists identically among committee insiders and outsiders. These findings suggest that winnowing is not content-neutral: institutions and content operate at different thresholds of the same pipeline, with consequences for whose interests the legislative process systematically disadvantages.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% ============================================================

When a legislature receives more bills than its committees can process, the resulting winnowing raises a fundamental question: does bill content shape which proposals survive? Theodore Lowi (1964) predicted that different policy types generate systematically different political dynamics. Redistributive legislation, which imposes identifiable costs on organized groups to benefit others, activates opposition that distributive legislation, which channels targeted benefits without mobilizing opponents, does not. If this asymmetry operates at the committee stage, where the vast majority of legislative proposals succeed or fail, then the institutional process of winnowing is not politically neutral. It systematically disadvantages policies serving populations who would benefit from redistribution.

Despite six decades of theoretical development, there exists, to my knowledge, no study that tests Lowi's prediction at the committee processing level in any legislature. The winnowing literature treats committee attrition as driven by capacity constraints and institutional procedures (Krutz 2005), without examining whether the content of legislation independently predicts processing outcomes. The legislative effectiveness literature measures individual legislators' ability to advance bills through the pipeline (Volden and Wiseman 2014; Bucchianeri, Volden, and Wiseman 2024), but conditions effectiveness on legislator attributes rather than policy type. And the committee organization literature debates whether committees serve informational (Krehbiel 1991), partisan (Cox and McCubbins 2005), or distributional purposes, without connecting these institutional theories to bill-level processing outcomes differentiated by content.

This lacuna may stem from two measurement challenges. First, classifying legislation by policy type at scale requires systematic content analysis that most bill-level datasets lack. Second, separating the content channel from the institutional channel requires data on sponsors' committee positions, which is rarely merged with bill-level outcome data. The Korean National Assembly (KNA) offers an unusual opportunity to address both challenges. Its bill-tracking system records detailed processing outcomes for every proposal, its propose-reason texts (\emph{제안이유}) permit keyword-based content classification, and its cosponsorship records allow construction of sponsor-committee matching variables.

I argue that committee processing operates through what I call a two-gate architecture. The first gate is institutional: whether the bill's sponsor serves on the committee that reviews it. Sponsors with committee membership can advocate through subcommittee participation, scheduling requests, and informal negotiation with the chair, advantages unavailable to outsiders. This gate is content-neutral; it raises processing probability by a similar magnitude for all bill types. The second gate is content-based: redistributive legislation generates organized opposition that creates friction at the committee stage, regardless of the sponsor's institutional position. This gate is institution-neutral; it imposes a processing penalty that committee membership cannot overcome.

To test this architecture, I classify 12,889 member-sponsored bills from the 20th and 21st KNA (2016 to 2024) into six content categories and estimate nested logistic regressions with committee fixed effects. Three findings emerge. First, committee membership is the single most powerful predictor of processing, with an average marginal effect roughly five times larger than any content variable. This extends Kim and Lee's (2023) finding that subcommittee position predicts bill passage in the KNA, showing that the institutional access premium operates even earlier in the pipeline, at the committee processing stage.

Second, the Lowi gradient, the gap between redistributive and distributive legislation within the livelihood policy domain, is substantial and remarkably stable. Labor bills process at rates roughly 18 percentage points below small-business bills, a gradient that survives every control including committee fixed effects, arrival timing, bill length, cosponsors, and, crucially, sponsor-committee membership. When I decompose the sample by committee insider status, the gradient is virtually identical for insiders and outsiders. This is the paper's central theoretical finding: institutional access determines which bills clear the first gate, but policy content determines relative processing rates at the second, and the two gates operate independently.

Third, the average content penalty, the gap between all livelihood (\emph{민생}) and non-livelihood legislation, is modest. It disappears entirely for committee insiders, suggesting that institutional access can overcome the mild friction associated with livelihood legislation as a broad category. The severe friction is concentrated within the livelihood domain, between redistributive and distributive subtypes. The distinction matters: it is not that livelihood legislation is uniformly disadvantaged, but that specific redistributive categories, particularly labor legislation, face structural processing barriers that neither institutional position nor sponsor effort can fully overcome.

I situate these findings within the committee organization literature by testing whether the institutional access premium reflects informational expertise (Krehbiel 1991) or partisan alignment (Cox and McCubbins 2005). The premium is flat across bill complexity quartiles, inconsistent with the informational theory's prediction that expertise should matter more for technically complex legislation. Korean evidence that committee composition reflects partisan rather than informational considerations (Choi and Koo 2018) and that committee chairs are selected for party loyalty (Kang 2023) favors the partisan interpretation, though the data cannot definitively distinguish the two.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the committee organization and policy typology literatures and derives the two-gate expectation. Section 3 describes the KNA data, keyword classification, and identification strategy. Section 4 presents results. Section 5 discusses implications, limitations, and connections to prior work. Section 6 concludes.

\section{Literature and Theory}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{The Winnowing Problem}

Legislatures that receive more proposals than they can process must winnow. Krutz (2005) demonstrates that the U.S. Congress processes a declining share of introduced bills over time, a pattern he attributes to institutional capacity constraints rather than deliberate content filtering. In his framework, committees triage based on heuristics such as timing and sponsor characteristics, and the resulting winnowing is largely content-neutral. The Korean National Assembly presents an extreme version of this problem: bill introductions tripled between the 17th and 21st Assemblies, while committee meeting days and staff resources grew far more slowly. Approximately 80 percent of failed bills in the 20th and 21st terms expired after placement on a standing committee agenda without ever receiving a committee decision, a pattern of death by inaction rather than active rejection.

Kim and Lee (2026) independently confirm that structural factors dominate individual legislator competence in explaining bill passage in the 19th through 21st KNA. Their analysis finds that procedural environment and deliberative frameworks are the decisive variables, with limited influence from legislator-level attributes. This structural emphasis aligns with the winnowing perspective but leaves open the question of whether bill content operates as an independent structural factor alongside institutional procedures.

\subsection{Committee Organization: Three Theories of Institutional Access}

The American legislative studies canon offers three competing theories of why committee membership should predict bill processing. Krehbiel's (1991) informational theory holds that committees provide expertise to the floor; members possess policy knowledge that helps them craft better legislation and navigate review procedures. Under this theory, the sponsor-committee match premium reflects an expertise dividend, and it should be larger for technically complex bills where informational advantages matter most.

Cox and McCubbins (2005) propose a partisan theory in which the majority party uses committee control to exercise negative agenda power. Under this theory, the committee membership premium reflects partisan alignment between the sponsor and committee leadership rather than expertise per se. Choi and Koo (2018) directly test these theories in the KNA and find that partisan considerations, not informational specialization, best explain committee composition in the 18th and 19th Assemblies. Committee members exhibit minimal ideological differences from co-partisans but significant differences from opposing-party members. Kang (2023) provides the complementary finding that party loyalty predicts selection to committee leadership positions in the 20th Assembly, suggesting that committee chairs are partisan agents, not neutral arbiters.

The distributional theory (Weingast and Marshall 1988) proposes that committees serve as vehicles for logrolling, with members claiming jurisdictional property rights over policy domains important to their constituencies. Under this theory, the committee membership premium reflects jurisdictional ownership rather than expertise or partisan alignment.

These three theories generate a shared prediction, that committee membership should substantially increase processing probability, but diverge on the mechanism. They also share a limitation: none predicts that the committee membership premium should vary by bill content. All three theories expect a uniform institutional advantage regardless of what the bill proposes. This uniformity assumption creates the theoretical space for the second gate.

\subsection{Lowi's Policy Typology and Committee-Level Friction}

Lowi (1964) argued that policy types generate distinct political dynamics. Redistributive policies, such as minimum wage legislation or progressive taxation, impose concentrated costs on organized groups (employers, high-income taxpayers) to produce diffuse benefits for less-organized populations (workers, low-income households). Distributive policies, such as small-business subsidies or infrastructure earmarks, channel targeted benefits without imposing identifiable costs. The asymmetry in political mobilization, Lowi predicted, should produce systematically different legislative trajectories.

Volden, Wiseman, and Wittmer (2016) provide the closest bill-level test of content-based processing differentials. They show that bills addressing women's issues in the U.S. Congress face lower passage rates than other legislation, a finding consistent with the Lowi prediction that policies benefiting less-organized constituencies encounter greater legislative friction. Peay (2020) demonstrates that this content penalty survives institutional position: Congressional Black Caucus members' targeted-issue bills face disproportionate processing losses even when those members hold relevant committee and subcommittee positions.

Peay's finding anticipates the two-gate architecture I propose, but with a critical difference. Peay cannot separate whether the processing penalty reflects the identity of the sponsor (Black lawmakers) or the content of the legislation (racial equity bills). The KNA data permits this separation, because the content penalty can be tested within the same sponsor's portfolio and across sponsors of all partisan and geographic types.

\subsection{The Two-Gate Expectation}

Synthesizing these literatures, I derive the following expectation. If committee processing is determined solely by institutional access (Gate 1), then the sponsor-committee match should predict processing outcomes, but bill content should add no explanatory power after controlling for institutional position. If processing is determined solely by content friction (Gate 2), then Lowi-type classifications should predict outcomes regardless of sponsor characteristics, but institutional access should be irrelevant. The two-gate architecture predicts that both channels operate simultaneously and independently: institutional access determines which bills clear the first threshold, while content determines relative processing rates conditional on clearing it. Formally, the institutional premium should be similar across content types (Gate 1 is content-neutral), and the content gradient should be similar across insider and outsider subsamples (Gate 2 is institution-neutral).

Jun and Hix (2010) provide an additional theoretical lever. They find that proportional representatives in the KNA demonstrate greater party independence than district members, a reversal of the pattern observed in most mixed-member systems. If proportional representatives are more policy-motivated and less inclined toward position-taking, their bill portfolios represent a cleaner test of content-based processing friction, because the position-taking alternative explanation is weakened. A content penalty that persists, or intensifies, among proportional representatives' bills would constitute strong evidence for the Lowi mechanism over the position-taking alternative.

\section{Data and Method}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Data}

The analysis draws on the Korean National Assembly bill database for the 20th (2016--2020) and 21st (2020--2024) terms. The universe includes all member-sponsored bills (\emph{의원발의 법률안}), excluding government-sponsored and committee-sponsored legislation, which follow distinct institutional pathways. From this universe, I extract 12,889 bills with sufficient propose-reason text (\emph{제안이유}) for content classification.

The dependent variable is \emph{committee decision}: whether the bill received any formal action from the standing committee to which it was referred, including approval, revision, rejection, or incorporation into an alternative bill (\emph{대안반영폐기}). Bills that expired without committee action are coded as zero. This is a broader outcome than passage or enactment; it captures whether the committee engaged with the bill at all. In the 20th and 21st Assemblies, approximately 35 percent of member-sponsored bills received a committee decision.

I classify bills into six content categories using keyword matching on propose-reason texts: \emph{Labor} (노동, 근로, 임금, 산재), \emph{Welfare} (복지, 기초생활, 장애인, 연금), \emph{Care} (돌봄, 보육, 요양, 아동), \emph{Small Business} (소상공인, 중소기업, 자영업, 소기업), \emph{Industry} (산업, 기업, 투자, 경제성장), and \emph{Safety} (안전, 재난, 사고, 환경). The first four categories constitute livelihood legislation (\emph{민생법안}); the last two are non-livelihood. Within the livelihood domain, I follow Lowi's typology in classifying Labor and Welfare as \emph{redistributive} (imposing costs on organized groups) and Care and Small Business as \emph{distributive} (channeling benefits without concentrated cost-bearers).

The key independent variable for Gate 1 is \emph{sponsor-committee match}: whether the bill's primary sponsor serves on the standing committee to which the bill is referred. I approximate committee membership by identifying each legislator's primary committee as the committee to which they most frequently sponsor bills. Among 12,889 bills, 37.8 percent show the sponsor serving on the bill's referred committee. This proxy introduces measurement error that likely attenuates the true committee membership effect.

Controls include months since the start of the Assembly term (capturing arrival timing effects), logged text length of the propose-reason (a proxy for bill seriousness), logged number of cosponsors, and committee and Assembly term fixed effects.

\subsection{Identification Strategy}

The main specification is a logistic regression of the form:

\begin{equation}
\Pr(\text{Decision}_i = 1) = \Lambda(\beta_1 \text{Minsaeng}_i + \beta_2 \text{CommitteeMatch}_i + \mathbf{X}_i \boldsymbol{\gamma} + \delta_c + \alpha_t + \epsilon_i)
\label{eq:main}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\Lambda(\cdot)$ is the logistic function, $\text{Minsaeng}_i$ indicates livelihood legislation, $\text{CommitteeMatch}_i$ indicates sponsor-committee membership, $\mathbf{X}_i$ is a vector of bill-level controls (arrival timing, text length, cosponsors), $\delta_c$ are committee fixed effects, and $\alpha_t$ are Assembly term fixed effects. The coefficient $\beta_1$ captures the content penalty net of institutional access, and $\beta_2$ captures the institutional premium net of content.

I estimate six nested models to trace how the minsaeng coefficient attenuates as controls are added, following the logic of coefficient stability tests (Oster 2019). I then decompose the sample by committee insider status and by Lowi subcategory to test the two-gate predictions: that the institutional premium is content-neutral (similar $\beta_2$ across content types) and that the content gradient is institution-neutral (similar Lowi gradient for insiders and outsiders).

The identification challenge is that bill content is not randomly assigned. Legislators choose what to propose, and content may correlate with unobserved bill quality, sponsor effort, or committee receptivity. Three design features mitigate this concern. First, committee fixed effects absorb committee-level heterogeneity in processing capacity and political orientation. Second, within-sponsor comparisons, exploiting the fact that many legislators introduce both livelihood and non-livelihood bills, control for time-invariant sponsor characteristics. Third, the Oster (2019) proportional selection diagnostic quantifies how much unobservable selection, relative to observable selection, would be needed to eliminate the content coefficient.

I do not claim causal identification in the strict quasi-experimental sense. The observational design supports the language of association and prediction rather than causal effect. What the design can establish is that the content gradient is not an artifact of institutional access, sponsor characteristics, or committee composition, the three most plausible alternative explanations.

Table~\ref{tab:main} presents six nested logistic regression models. The institutional access variable, sponsor-committee match, is the single most powerful predictor of committee processing. Several additional patterns merit attention. Arrival timing is the second most important predictor: bills introduced in the first year of the Assembly term receive committee decisions at substantially higher rates than those introduced in the final year, consistent with a capacity-driven winnowing process where early arrivals claim limited committee attention. Bill text length, a proxy for seriousness, is positively associated with processing. \subsection{Geographic Uniformity and the Position-Taking Alternative}

A natural concern is that the content penalty reflects position-taking rather than genuine policy friction. If legislators introduce livelihood bills for electoral credit without intending to advance them, the processing gap would reflect sponsor behavior rather than committee-level content discrimination. Six pieces of evidence argue against this interpretation.

First, the within-sponsor test controls for legislator-level propensity to introduce position-taking bills. The penalty operates within the same legislator's portfolio. Second, the penalty is geographically uniform: it ranges from approximately four to five percentage points across stronghold, swing, cross-party, and proportional-tier legislators (Table~\ref{tab:within}, lower panel). If position-taking drove the penalty, it should vary by district competitiveness, since safe-seat legislators face weaker electoral incentives for position-taking. It does not.

Third, and perhaps most compellingly, proportional representatives in the KNA, who are freed from district-level constituency service and are generally understood as more policy-oriented (Jun and Hix 2010), sponsor the most livelihood-heavy bill portfolios (68 percent livelihood versus 49 percent from stronghold legislators). Their Labor bills have the lowest committee decision rate in the entire dataset, approximately 25 percent. If position-taking inflated the livelihood bill pool, proportional representatives, with stronger policy motivations, should produce higher-quality livelihood bills that face a smaller penalty. Instead, the penalty persists or intensifies. This finding suggests that what Labor bills propose, not who proposes them, generates the processing friction.

Fourth, the committee-match premium is flat across bill complexity quartiles, ranging narrowly from approximately 13 to 15 percentage points regardless of bill text length. This uniformity is inconsistent with the informational theory's prediction (Krehbiel 1991) and more consistent with a partisan or procedural access interpretation. Choi and Koo's (2018) finding that KNA committee composition is partisan rather than informational aligns with this pattern.

Fifth, cosponsor count, which should predict processing if collective legislative effort matters, carries no predictive power in any specification. This null persists across all stages of this project and suggests that cosponsorship in the KNA operates differently from the U.S. Congress, where cosponsor counts signal coalition breadth.

Sixth, a legislator fixed-effects model that absorbs all time-invariant sponsor characteristics still yields a significant, albeit smaller, minsaeng coefficient, confirming that the content penalty is not an artifact of sponsor-level heterogeneity.


%% ============================================================
\section{Discussion}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Theoretical Implications}

These findings suggest a revision to how the winnowing and committee organization literatures understand bill attrition. Krutz (2005) treated winnowing as a capacity-driven process; the results here indicate that winnowing is structured by both institutional access and policy content, operating at different thresholds. The two-gate architecture reconciles the committee organization debate (which theory explains committee behavior?) with the policy typology literature (does content matter for legislative outcomes?) by showing that both traditions capture real variation in the same process.

The institutional access finding confirms and extends recent Korean scholarship. Kim and Lee (2023) showed that subcommittee position predicts bill passage; I show that the effect operates even earlier, at the processing stage, and with a larger magnitude, roughly 14 percentage points. Park (2026) critiques the direct-referral system to subcommittees (\emph{소위원회 직회부}) as concentrating legislative power; the institutional access premium documented here quantifies the extent of that concentration.

The Lowi gradient provides what appears to be the first committee-stage bill-level test of the redistributive-distributive prediction. Peay (2020) comes closest in the U.S. context, showing that Congressional Black Caucus members' targeted-issue bills face disproportionate processing losses even from committee positions of authority. I extend Peay's insight from identity-based to content-based friction, grounded in Lowi's canonical typology. The KNA data permits a cleaner separation of content from sponsor identity because the Lowi gradient operates within a single policy domain (livelihood legislation) and across sponsors of all geographic and partisan types.

The finding that the institutional premium is flat across bill complexity quartiles is inconsistent with pure informational theory and more consistent with partisan or procedural access interpretations. However, without data on committee chair party affiliation, which has not been available for this analysis, I cannot definitively distinguish the partisan mechanism (Cox and McCubbins 2005) from the procedural access mechanism. This remains the paper's most important unresolved interpretive question. Future work incorporating committee chair party data could determine whether the premium reflects co-partisan alignment or the procedural advantages of subcommittee participation.

\subsection{Magnitude and Substantive Significance}

The average minsaeng penalty of approximately three percentage points is modest in absolute terms. In a universe where baseline processing rates are 33 to 40 percent, three percentage points represents a reduction of roughly seven to nine percent. This is smaller than Volden, Wiseman, and Wittmer's (2016) finding for women's issues in the U.S. Congress, where the passage rate differential is proportionally larger. However, the comparison across different institutional contexts and outcome measures (processing versus passage) limits direct comparison.

The normative significance of the content penalty may exceed its statistical magnitude. The three-percentage-point average masks a much larger within-domain gradient: Labor bills process at roughly 18 points below Small Business bills. This gradient means that legislation addressing minimum wages, workplace safety, and union rights faces substantially greater committee friction than legislation addressing small-business subsidies and entrepreneurship support. To the extent that these content categories map onto the interests of different constituencies, organized labor versus small business owners, the committee pipeline systematically disadvantages one group's policy priorities relative to the other's.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations warrant caution. First, the sponsor-committee match variable is a proxy based on sponsorship frequency patterns rather than official committee membership rosters. Measurement error likely attenuates the true institutional access effect, meaning the reported premium of 14 percentage points is a lower bound. The insider-outsider split, which is a main finding, could be sharpened with official committee and subcommittee rosters.

Second, the keyword classifier covers approximately 29 percent of member-sponsored bills. The unclassified 71 percent includes bills using different vocabulary for similar policy domains. While the Oster diagnostic provides reassurance against omitted variable bias at the minsaeng level, the within-minsaeng Lowi gradient has not been subjected to the same formal robustness test. Misclassification would compress the gradient toward zero, suggesting the observed gap is a conservative estimate, but formal validation against a hand-coded sample remains desirable.

Third, committee chair party affiliation, flagged as a critical variable throughout this project, has not been obtained. The bilateral finding from the labor committee, where both governing and opposition party members' processing rates collapsed under divided government, weakens the simple partisan gatekeeping interpretation but does not resolve the question.

Fourth, the divided-government interaction that appeared robust in earlier specifications collapsed to non-significance when the committee-match control was added. The interaction may be committee-specific, concentrated in the labor committee where a raw difference-in-differences of roughly 20 percentage points appears between unified and divided government. But the non-minsaeng comparison group in that committee under divided government is too small (approximately 20 bills) to support formal causal identification. The divided-government finding should be treated as suggestive rather than established.

Fifth, this is an observational study. Bill content is endogenous to legislative strategy, and no instrument or natural experiment cleanly identifies the causal effect of content on processing. The within-sponsor design and coefficient stability diagnostics mitigate the most plausible confounders, but they cannot fully substitute for exogenous variation.


%% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%% ============================================================

I set out to test whether bill content predicts committee processing outcomes, and if so, whether the content channel operates independently of institutional access. The answer to both questions appears to be yes. Committee membership is the dominant predictor of processing, a finding consistent with recent Korean scholarship on subcommittee gatekeeping (Kim and Lee 2023; Park 2026) and with the broader committee organization literature's emphasis on institutional position. But content matters at the margin, and the margin is theoretically consequential. The Lowi gradient, the gap between redistributive labor bills and distributive small-business bills, is substantial, geographically invariant, and robust to institutional access controls. It persists identically among committee insiders and outsiders, suggesting that redistributive friction is structurally embedded in the committee process rather than mediated by sponsor-level characteristics.

The two-gate architecture I propose, where institutional access and policy content operate at different thresholds of the same pipeline, offers a synthesis that neither the committee organization nor the policy typology literature has articulated on its own. It suggests that winnowing is not the politically neutral capacity adjustment that Krutz (2005) described, but neither is it the pure content filtering that a strong reading of Lowi would predict. Both channels matter, at different levels. A comprehensive account of which bills survive the committee stage requires attending to both the institutional position of the sponsor and the redistributive character of the legislation.

The practical implications extend beyond the KNA. If content-based processing friction is a general feature of committee systems, then legislative institutions may systematically underprocess redistributive legislation in any context where committees face capacity constraints. This would represent a structural mechanism through which legislative institutions contribute to what Hacker (2004) calls policy drift: the maintenance of a policy status quo not through active resistance but through institutional inaction that selectively affects proposals for change.

Several avenues for future research emerge. Official committee and subcommittee membership data would sharpen the insider-outsider decomposition and permit a more precise test of whether the institutional premium reflects partisan alignment or procedural access. Merging bill-level data with electoral margin data could test whether district competitiveness operates as an instrument for bill seriousness, as suggested by recent work on Korean electoral geography. And extending the content classification to earlier Assembly terms would permit a time-series analysis of whether the Lowi gradient has widened as bill volume has grown, testing whether capacity constraints amplify content-based filtering.
